1 Introduction

This is an updated, experimental version of lalüta, a language which I have created in 2024. Since its creation, I have had several ideas on how I would have made things different if I were to redo the language from scratch. These ideas will be explored here, hopefully blossoming into a fully-fledged language which I'll be happier with than with its original form.

Lalüta is a personal constructed language. It does not attempt to be naturalistic, it is not set in any fictional world, nor does it have a fictional group of speakers. It is free of loan words and makes no reference to names of countries, people, brands, culture-specific ideas, etc. existing in the real world. Its purpose is self-expression, not creating or translating specialized or technical texts.

As this is a personal language, its "flavor," that is idiomatic expressions, register, and anything that in a natural language would be culture-guided, is based on my own thoughts, experiences, and worldviews. The language is not inspired by any particular language, natural or fictional, though it draws bits and pieces from many.

Although the language is not naturalistic, it features elements such as non-concatenation, proto-forms, grammaticalization, suppletion, and semantic shift, in order to make it more interesting. The language is exclusively head-initial, VSO and low-context, though it encourages the use of figurative and metaphoric expressions.

Due to the complexity of the language and the length of this document, mistakes in spelling, grammar and syntax in the presented examples are bound to be present. This is why I provide thorough explanations of the discussed grammatical, morphological, syntactical, etc. constructions and strategies. With the use of these descriptions, potential mistakes can be corrected.

2 Phonology


2.1 Vowels

The vowels are divided into front and back vowels. There is also one neutral vowel. This influences the realization of preceding consonants and the forms of some adjacent morphological units. Non-low vowels can be realized as glides when found next to other vowels (see 2.5 Vowel hiatus). Note that ä and a have the same pronunciation, but the former is front while the latter is back.

Front ä e i a e i /a ɛ i/
Back a o u a o u /a ʌ ɯ/
Neutral y ü /y/
Glides i/e u/o y y/i w/u ẅ/ü /j ɰ ɥ/

Glides are romanized as y w ẅ word-initially, intervocalically, and after high vowels (in sequences iw iẅ uy), and i u ü otherwise. Geminate glides are romanized as iy uw üẅ.

2.3 Realization and allophony

Onset voiceless stops are aspirated, especially when stressed. Aspiration before glides results in their devoicing, e.g. poa pua [pɰ̊a].
/ɡ/ is realized as [ŋ] between vowels and glides.

Coda /ɰ̃/ lowers high vowels - iN yN en /ɛɰ̃/, uN on /ʌɰ̃/.
/ɰ̃/ is realized as [ɥ̃] before /pj vj/ and [j̃] before /ʃ ʒ tʃ/.
Coda /m/ and /n/ assimilate to following obstruents: [m] before /p/, [ɱ] before /v/, [n] before /t d s/, [ɲ] before /ʃ ʒ/, [ŋ] before /k ɡ/. They are romanized as m before p, and n otherwise, e.g. ainva ainva [ajɱva], eynce eünche [ɛɥɲʃɛ], ounc ounc [ʌɰŋk].
Coda /ɹ/ influences the realization of some preceding vowels and glides - er or ur ur /ɚ/, yr ür /ɥɚ/, (V)ir (V)ür (V)ur /jɚ ɥɚ ɰɚ/. In the latter case, the additional rhotic vowel is purely epenthetic and does not count as a separate syllable nucleus for the purposes of counting word stress, cf. parer payur /paˈjɚ/ and pair pair /ˈpajɚ/. Non-epenthetic /jɚ ɥɚ ɰɚ/ are romanized as yur yür wur.

Geminates are always realized as geminates, never as doubled consonants, e.g. appa appa [apːa], not [*ap.pa]. For the purposes of word stress counting, syllables preceding a geminate are closed.

/ɯ/ is realized as [ɯ̟~ɨ]. It is slightly rhotacized before a pause.
/a/ is realized as [ä] following hard consonants, as [a] following soft consonants, and as [æ] before /ɹ/.

Two glides cannot cooccur adjacently within the same syllable. The latter is elided, e.g. lail /*ɰajɰ/ → /ɰaj/ wai.

ri lu are pronounced /ji ɰɯ/ after vowels and /i ɯ/ otherwise, e.g. luca uca /ɯka/, rion yon /jʌɰ̃/, ari ayi /aji/, ellu ewu /ɛɰɯ/.

Word boundaries typically block palatalization, excluding certain set phrases or collocations; this also affects romanization, e.g. läl üta lal üta.

2.4 Stress and phonotactics

Word stress is final if the syllable is closed, otherwise penultimate if the syllable is closed, otherwise antepenultimate. Every other preceding syllable receives secondary stress. In case of exceptions, the stress may be marked with an acute in the romanization, but this is generally reserved for didactic tools like dictionaries. The exceptions to the stress rule include:

  • - compound SDOAUX, which are always stressed on the final syllable, e.g. arärä ayayá
  • - second person verb forms ending in -ciu, which are always stressed on the penult, e.g. eliciu elíchu
  • - adjectives and nouns involved in NSWEO agreement, e.g. vi oli vi olí, elca elboa eucá euvuá
  • - certain short compounds, where stress can be counted as if two words were used, e.g. läl'yta lalűta (from läl yta lal üta)
  • - certain clipped compounds, e.g. cada cadá (from ca darn ca darn)

The following consonant clusters are allowed:

  • - word-initially: sP
  • - word-medially: Pː sP ss sm sn sr WsP* WC*
  • - word-finally: sP WP* Ws* WsP* LN are allowed

*Nasals must be homorganic with the following consonant: only m is allowed before p, only n is allowed otherwise.

Any instances of word-initial or word-final sb sd sg are respelled as sp st sc, e.g. s- + -darc → starc, pag + -s- → pasc.

2.7 Umlaut

Umlaut involves the mutation of a vowel or a vowel sequence when it is followed by an appended front high vowel. This can occur when an inflectional or derivational affix is attached to a word, and in SDOAUX (see 4.4 Clause-final auxiliaries). It does not apply between morphemes in compounds or across word boundaries, so articles, demonstratives and adjectives do not trigger umlaut.

An affixed i changes the preceding vowel - ä a e o u → e ä i e i, e.g. so- GEN + mica thing → semica of thing (GEN-thing).
An affixed y changes the preceding vowel or combination thereof - ä a e o u → e ä y y y, e.g. es put + (-y) GER → ys putting (put-GER).
Umlaut does not apply when there is no intervening consonant, e.g. rättä teach + i GER → rättäi teaching (teach-GER).

Umlaut affects digraphs as follows. When more than two vowels are in a row, only the final two are affected.

2.8 Reduplication

Some forms in the language are formed through reduplication, which adheres to the following patterns:

  • - if the word consists of only a vowel or digraph, r (before front vowels) or l (before back vowels) is inserted and the original sequence is repeated, e.g. a → ala, eu → eureu
  • - if the word ends in more than two vowels, the final two vowels are repeated, e.g. uae → uaeae, caio → caioio
  • - otherwise if the word ends with a vowel, the final CV(s) are repeated, with voicing of the reduplicated consonant (p t c → v d g), e.g. poa → poavoa, sti → stidi
  • - if the word ends in a single consonant, the final V(s)C are repeated, with voicing of the reduplicated consonant, e.g. ac → agac, staim → staimaim
  • - if the word ends in multiple consonants, the final V(s)Cs are repeated, e.g. reinsc → reinsceinsc, sporn → spornorn

4 Overarching structures


4.1 Class

Verbs, adjectives and nouns are divided into one of five classes named after cardinal directions; a system referred to as NSWEO. This is similar to grammatical gender, though gender is typically associated with agreement and intrinsic categorization, while this system involves harmony and semantic associations. Combinations of words within the same class are considered semantically stronger and may be used in idioms and collocations. Adjective endings are changed to rhyme with that of the noun if they have the same class. The only form of class agreement is seen in demonstratives and articles.

There are third person pronouns for each of the five classes, though animate referents are not bound to a single class. The classes rather suggest certain features or traits, so the same referent may switch class depending on the context determined by the judgment of the speaker, e.g. a person seen as good by the speaker may be S, while someone perceived as an introvert may be N. Inanimate referents typically stay within one class, though the class may be changed for metaphoric reasons. Each class has a number of possible endings, though exceptions exist.

Class Name Examples of associations Endings Rare endings
North (N) pil piw cold, dark, bad, calm, quiet, still, weak L*
South (S) rio yo hot, bright, good, lively, loud, move, strong B e o i u
West (W) avi avi end, old, death, short, hear P ä i e o
East (E) mon mon start, new, young, life, long, say N* u y
Neutral (O) püta püta human, nature s* y a e o

The rare readings typically come from contractions of double vowels, e.g. vee → vi (S) light.

There are a few grammatical words belonging to the O class with the ending ä, including gä this, lä what and pronouns ending in either of the two, as well as all other interrogatives and demonstratives. Several indefinite pronouns which use the negative article as an enclitic retain their class, ignoring the ending of the article, e.g. cusan is O, not E.

There are several clipped compounds which retain their class despite clipping, for example cusca same person (S because it is a clipped form of cus cago).

◆ North and south

The north class (gata ci spil gata chi spiw) is found on the value axis (rotoyt a spipa notoüt a spipa) along with the south class. North words are low in value and associated with concepts such as bad, darkness, cold and weakness. As such, north is found at the bottom of the compass. Apart from negative concepts, north is also related to silence, serenity and stillness. A person described as north could be perceived as cold or indifferent, but the class can also be ascribed to someone quiet, introverted, logical, or calm and collected.

The south class (gata ci serio gata chi sieyo) is found on the opposite side of the value axis. South is related to concepts opposite of those classified as north - good, light, heat, and strength. It is found at the top of the compass. Other south concepts include movement, passion and lust. A south person is typically extroverted, open, passionate, and may be turbulent and easily swayed by emotions.

◆ West and east

The west class (gata ci s'avi gata chi savi) is found on the existence axis (rotoyt a s'adavois notoüt a sadavois) along with the east class. West signifies finality, death, shortness and destruction. It is also associated with features such as stoicism and independence. West also conveys concreteness and action. The class is found on the right side of the compass. A west person is perceived as a realist, or someone who prefers acting based on observing the world around them. Those who enjoy conflict or competition can also be described as west.

The east class (gata ci somon gata chi somon) is found on the opposite side of the existence axis and on the left side of the compass. It is associated with genesis, life, longevity and creation. Traits such as kindness, nurturing and care are also east. Since the class is associated with creation, it encompasses abstract concepts and focuses on thought rather than action. East is the class of all artists and those sensitive to beauty, as well as people seen as kind, agreeable and caring.

◆ Neutral

The neutral class (gata ci spyta gata chi spüta) is placed in the middle of the compass and is the balance to the remaining four classes. All neutral concepts are covered by this class. This includes generic words such as nature, type, shape, image, place and sound. Among neutral words are also those which balance out a north/south or west/east pair, such as temperature (heat + cold). The neutral class can be ascribed to any person and is used to remove any emotional association with the referent. As such, it can be used when addressing a group of people, since those are highly unlikely to be homogeneous, or an unknown person (e.g. in questionnaires). The neutral pronoun is also the pronominal equivalent of indefinite pronouns such as cusan no one/someone and mican nothing/something.

4.2 Number

Nouns, as well as first and second person pronouns inflect for number using an animacy-based system. Nouns are divided into one of five classes, each having a default, "expected" number which is unmarked, and an "unexpected" number which is marked identically across all animacy classes (via reduplication). These classes are independent of NSWEO.

Class Unmarked Marked
1 unique nouns nid (the) sun ninid suns, (a) sun
2 nouns coming in sets pyi (a pair of) eyes pyivyi (an) eye, (many) eyes
3 other animate nouns cus person cugus people
4 mass nouns nal water nanal (a glass of) water, (a) water
5 other inanimate nouns saecuic murder saecuguic (a) murder

Class 1 nouns comprises unique referents, such as nid the sun, vis the world, as well as numeric nouns like sava two, vota thousand. For the former group, the nouns are typically accompanied by the definite article, e.g. nid a the sun. Class 1 nouns are marked when the instance does not refer to a unique referent, e.g. ninid suns, vivis r'än my world.

Class 2 nouns are those which typically occur in a specific number, e.g. days of the week (7), seasons (4), eyes (2), parents (2). They are unmarked when referred to as a whole set, and marked when referring to only one member of the set, or when occurring in any other unexpected number, cf. pyi (a pair of) eyes, pyivyi an eye, more than two eyes. Because they are expected to occur in a specific number, class 1 and 2 nouns are collectively referred to as set nouns.

Class 3 nouns are any other animate nouns - people or animals. They are unmarked when singular and marked when plural, e.g. cus person, cugus people.

The remaining three classes are collectively referred to as inanimate nouns. Class 4 nouns are concrete mass nouns, and are unmarked by default and marked when specifying a unit or a specific instance, e.g. nal water → nanal a glass/bottle/unit of water, stag fire → stadag a fire (a campfire/fire on a match, etc.).

Class 4 nouns comprise abstract concepts. They are unmarked when referring to them as general concepts and marked when referring to specific instances of them, e.g. saekuik murder → Neo saekuguik ari. There's been a murder., kaio love → Äb'et kaioio diibi misan usa läläb. Our love will never die.

Class 5 nouns are other inanimate nouns. If abstract, they are unmarked when referring to them as general concepts and marked when referring to specific instances of them, e.g. saekuik murder → Neo saekuguik ari. There's been a murder., kaio love → Äb'et kaioio diibi misan usa läläb. Our love will never die. If concrete, they are ambiguous to number and the marked form is applied only to explicitly specify one of something. The form is generally only used in a contrastive sense to resolve potential ambiguities, or when a plurality is expected, e.g. paiman cookie(s) → Peobeoms paimaman nänon. I baked a cookie. (normally people make more).

4.3 Case

There are three cases - direct (nominative + accusative: marks subjects and direct objects), genitive (marks possessors, doubles as ablative) and dative (marks indirect objects, doubles as locative). The genitive and dative cases are also used in conjunction with certain prepositions. The cases may be applied to nouns, pronouns and adjectives, the latter agreeing in case with the noun they describe. However, case prefixes are also applied to verbs to form a number of tenses - the genitive conveys the meaning of past, while the dative conveys the meaning of future/irrealis.

The direct case is unmarked, while genitive and dative marking depends on the animacy of the noun. Animate nouns (class 3), non-set personal pronouns, as well as all verbs are marked with one set of prefixes, inanimate nouns (class 4 and 5) are marked with another, and set nouns (class 1 and 2) and pronouns are marked with yet another set. Attributive adjectives take the same set of prefixes as the nouns they describe, cf. semica s'olo of good thing (GEN-thing GEN-good) and nicus r'olo of good person (GEN-person GEN-good). Gerunds take the inanimate prefixes when marked for case, since the animate prefixes are used to indicate tense, e.g. piomu doing (do-GER) → äppiomu going to do (gerund of future form) (AN.DAT-do-GER), ilpiomu at doing (dative of gerund) (INAN.DAT-do-GER).

The inanimate forms are the default ones used whenever applying case prefixes to items other than nouns, adjectives or verbs.

4.4 Clause-final auxiliaries

Each clause ends with an auxiliary word which encodes the clause's subject and direct object (if there is one), in that order. This is referred to as SDOAUX (Subject Direct Object AUXiliary) and is done using personal pronouns in specific forms. Each pronoun has a default form, which is used in isolation or in SDOAUX in clauses without a direct object. Each pronoun also has a prefixing subject form and a suffixing direct object form. If the subject or direct object in the clause is a pronoun, it only appears in SDOAUX, but if it is a noun, it appears inside the clause in the direct case and is reinforced in SDOAUX using a corresponding pronoun. See 7 Personal pronouns and Appendix 1: SDOAUX.

  • Pasc nä. I go. (go-1P 1SG.)
  • Saesc nälu. I kill you. (go-1P 1SG-2SG.)
  • Saec ecci arän. A man kills me. (kill-3P man 3WUM-1SG.)
  • Saesc ecci närä. I kill a man. (kill-1P man 1SG-3WUM.)
  • Saec monc an miion. A woman kills a child. (kill-3P woman child 3EUM-3EUM.)
  • Saec an monc miion. A child kills a woman. (kill-3P child woman 3EUM-3EUM.)

Because both subjects and direct objects take the direct case, some ambiguities may arise and must be resolved through context, e.g. Saegaec cus papa. (kill-REDUP-3P person 3OUM-3OUM.) can mean He killed the person., or The person killed him.

SDOAUX are omitted when the verb in the clause is a copula or a locative copula and the subject is a first or second person pronoun, since these verbs conjugate for number as well as for person. The same is true for SDOAUX which repeat in subsequent clauses - only the final one must be used, provided that the pronouns apply to the same referents.

  • Sion emi. I am here. (LOC.COP-1SG DAT-here.)
  • Ar emi pa. It is here. (LOC.COP-3P DAT-here 3OUM.)
  • Ägä carst, mun nä. I lose, but I learn. (but lose-1P, learn-1P 1SG.)
  • Orsaesc monc a näion, saesc an cän näion. If I kill the woman, I kill the child. (GEN-kill-1SG woman DEF.ART 1SG-3EUM, kill-1SG child DEF.ART 1SG-3EUM.)

There are cases of multiple subjects or direct objects in a clause (x and y, x or y). If pronouns are included therein, they are not omitted in favor of SDOAUX. The SDOAUX in such cases always takes the plural (1P and 2P) or marked (3P) form, with the NSWEO determined by the first noun mentioned.

  • Äscest ä nä, ciulu si. You and me are going to die. (DAT-die-1P and 1SG, 2SG 1PL.)
  • Elliciu dar ciulu, dar ecci co la. You or the man knows. (know-2P or 2SG, or man DEF.ART 2PL.)
  • Eli ä ecci, monc e leyt, ugu ariri. A man and a woman see a tree and a house. (see-3P and man, woman and tree, house 3WM-3WM.)

Some syncretism within compound SDOAUX occurs, which must be disambiguated through context or spelling, e.g. 3OM-3OUM pav- + -pa → pappa pappá, 3OM-3OM pav- + -va → pappa pappá.
