1 Introduction

This is an updated, experimental version of lalüta, a language which I have created in 2024. Since its creation, I have had several ideas on how I would have made things different if I were to redo the language from scratch. These ideas will be explored here, hopefully blossoming into a fully-fledged language which I'll be happier with than with its original form.

Lalüta is a personal constructed language. It does not attempt to be naturalistic, it is not set in any fictional world, nor does it have a fictional group of speakers. It is free of loan words and makes no reference to names of countries, people, brands, culture-specific ideas, etc. existing in the real world. Its purpose is self-expression, not creating or translating specialized or technical texts.

As this is a personal language, its "flavor," that is idiomatic expressions, register, and anything that in a natural language would be culture-guided, is based on my own thoughts, experiences, and worldviews. The language is not inspired by any particular language, natural or fictional, though it draws bits and pieces from many.

Although the language is not naturalistic, it features elements such as non-concatenation, proto-forms, grammaticalization, suppletion, and semantic shift, in order to make it more interesting. The language is exclusively head-initial, VSO and low-context, though it encourages the use of figurative and metaphoric expressions.

Due to the complexity of the language and the length of this document, mistakes in spelling, grammar and syntax in the presented examples are bound to be present. This is why I provide thorough explanations of the discussed grammatical, morphological, syntactical, etc. constructions and strategies. With the use of these descriptions, potential mistakes can be corrected.

2 Phonology


2.1 Vowels

The vowels are divided into front and back vowels. There is also one neutral vowel. This influences the realization of preceding consonants and the forms of some adjacent morphological units. Non-low vowels can be realized as glides when found next to other vowels (see 2.5 Vowel hiatus). Note that ä and a have the same pronunciation, but the former is front while the latter is back.

Front ä e i a e i /a ɛ i/
Back a o u a o u /a ʌ ɯ/
Neutral y ü /y/
Glides i/e u/o y y/i w/u ẅ/ü /j ɰ ɥ/

Glides are romanized as y w ẅ word-initially, intervocalically, and after high vowels (in sequences iw iẅ uy), and i u ü otherwise. Geminate glides are romanized as iy uw üẅ.

2.2 Consonants

Consonants have a "hard" (non-palatalized) pronunciation before back/nautral vowels and glides, and a "soft" (palatalized) pronunciation before front vowels and glides. In coda position, obstruents are realized the same way as hard consonants, whereas more variability can be observed in sonorants.

d d /d/ j /ʒ/ d /d/

g g /ɡ/ j /ʒ/ g /ɡ/
s s /s/ si /sj/ s /s/

2.3 Realization and allophony

Onset voiceless stops are aspirated, especially when stressed. Aspiration before glides results in their devoicing, e.g. poa pua [pɰ̊a].
/ɡ/ is realized as [ŋ] between vowels and glides.

Coda /ɰ̃/ lowers high vowels - iN yN en /ɛɰ̃/, uN on /ʌɰ̃/.
/ɰ̃/ is realized as [ɥ̃] before /pj vj/ and [j̃] before /ʃ ʒ tʃ/.
Coda /m/ and /n/ assimilate to following obstruents: [m] before /p/, [ɱ] before /v/, [n] before /t d s/, [ɲ] before /ʃ ʒ/, [ŋ] before /k ɡ/. They are romanized as m before p, and n otherwise, e.g. ainva ainva [ajɱva], eynce eünche [ɛɥɲʃɛ], ounc ounc [ʌɰŋk].
Coda /ɹ/ influences the realization of some preceding vowels and glides - er or ur ur /ɚ/, yr ür /ɥɚ/, (V)ir (V)ür (V)ur /jɚ ɥɚ ɰɚ/. In the latter case, the additional rhotic vowel is purely epenthetic and does not count as a separate syllable nucleus for the purposes of counting word stress, cf. parer payur /paˈjɚ/ and pair pair /ˈpajɚ/. Non-epenthetic /jɚ ɥɚ ɰɚ/ are romanized as yur yür wur.

Geminates are always realized as geminates, never as doubled consonants, e.g. appa appa [apːa], not [*ap.pa]. For the purposes of word stress counting, syllables preceding a geminate are closed.

/ɯ/ is realized as [ɯ̟~ɨ]. It is slightly rhotacized before a pause.
/a/ is realized as [ä] following hard consonants, as [a] following soft consonants, and as [æ] before /ɹ/.

Two glides cannot cooccur adjacently within the same syllable. The latter is elided, e.g. lail /*ɰajɰ/ → /ɰaj/ wai.

ri lu are pronounced /ji ɰɯ/ after vowels and /i ɯ/ otherwise, e.g. luca uca /ɯka/, rion yon /jʌɰ̃/, ari ayi /aji/, ellu ewu /ɛɰɯ/.

Word boundaries typically block palatalization, excluding certain set phrases or collocations; this also affects romanization, e.g. läl üta lal üta.

2.4 Stress and phonotactics

Word stress is final if the syllable is closed, otherwise penultimate if the syllable is closed, otherwise antepenultimate. Every other preceding syllable receives secondary stress. In case of exceptions, the stress may be marked with an acute in the romanization, but this is generally reserved for didactic tools like dictionaries. The exceptions to the stress rule include:

  • - compound SDOAUX, which are always stressed on the final syllable, e.g. arärä ayayá
  • - second person verb forms ending in -ciu, which are always stressed on the penult, e.g. eliciu elíchu
  • - adjectives and nouns involved in NSWEO agreement, e.g. vi oli vi olí, elca elboa eucá euvuá
  • - certain short compounds, where stress can be counted as if two words were used, e.g. läl'yta lalűta (from läl yta lal üta)
  • - certain clipped compounds, e.g. cada cadá (from ca darn ca darn)

The following consonant clusters are allowed:

  • - word-initially: sP
  • - word-medially: Pː sP ss sm sn sr WsP* WC*
  • - word-finally: sP WP* Ws* WsP* LN are allowed

*Nasals must be homorganic with the following consonant: only m is allowed before p, only n is allowed otherwise.

Any instances of word-initial or word-final sb sd sg are respelled as sp st sc, e.g. s- + -darc → starc, pag + -s- → pasc.

  • - the apostrophe in the table above marks palatalization of the preceding consonant, e.g. mäe miai
  • - i is never pronounced as /j/ after consonants, rather it palatalizes the preceding consonant, e.g. cia /*ʃja/ → /ʃa/ cha

If more than two vowels are used in a row, they must alternate between glides and vowels, either starting with a glide (e.g. coae quai, dioie joye), or starting with a vowel (e.g. maio mayo, suuae suwai).

2.7 Umlaut

Umlaut involves the mutation of a vowel or a vowel sequence when it is followed by an appended front high vowel. This can occur when an inflectional or derivational affix is attached to a word, and in SDOAUX (see 4.4 Clause-final auxiliaries). It does not apply between morphemes in compounds or across word boundaries, so articles, demonstratives and adjectives do not trigger umlaut.

An affixed i changes the preceding vowel or combination thereof - ä a e o u → e ä i e i, e.g. so- GEN + mika thing → semika of thing (GEN-thing).
An affixed ü changes the preceding vowel or combination thereof - ä a e o u → e ä ü ü ü, e.g. does feel + -ü GER → düüsü feeling (feel-GER).
Umlaut does not apply when there is no intervening consonant, e.g. rättä teach + i GER → rättäi teaching (teach-GER).

This is a simple introduction to umlaut. For more details, see 17.9 Umlaut.
