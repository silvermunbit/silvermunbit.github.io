1 Introduction

This is a personal constructed language. Its name is läl'üta lalǘta, which comes directly from the word meaning language. It does not attempt to be naturalistic, it is not set in any fictional world, nor does it have a fictional group of speakers. It is free of loan words and makes no reference to names of countries, people, brands, culture-specific ideas, etc. existing in our world. Its purpose is to express ideas universal to the human experience, rather than to create or translate specialized or technical texts.

As this is a personal language, its "flavor," that is idiomatic expressions, register, and anything that in a natural language would be culture-guided, is based on my own thoughts, experiences, and worldview. The language is not inspired by any particular language, natural or fictional, though it draws bits and pieces from many.

Although the language is not naturalistic, it features elements such as non-concatenation, proto-forms, grammaticalization, suppletion, and the occassional semantic shift, in order to make it more interesting. The language is exclusively head initial, VSO and low-context, though it encourages the use of figurative and metaphoric language.

Some of the presented examples make use of made-up forms, not actual words.

Be wary that there are almost certainly some mistakes in either spelling, grammar or syntax (of the conlang) within this document.

The vowels are divided into front and back vowels. This influences the realization of preceding consonants and the forms of some adjacent morphological units. /ɡ/ is realized as [ŋ] intervocalically.

/s/ is realized as [ç] before soft consonants and romanized as h.

Coda /ɰ̃/ lowers high vowels - iN üN eũ /ɛɰ̃/, uN oũ /ʌɰ̃/.
/ɰ̃/ is realized as [ɥ̃] before /pʲ vʲ/ and [j̃] before /c ɟ ç/.

Coda r influences the realization of some preceding vowels and glides - er or ur /ɚ/, ür /ɥɚ/, yr ẅr wr /jɚ ɥɚ ɰɚ/. In the latter case, the additional rhotic vowel is purely epenthetic and does not count as a separate syllable nucleus for the purposes of counting word stress.

Geminates are always realized as geminates, never as doubled consonants, e.g. appa appa [apːa], not [*ap.pa]. For the purposes of word stress, syllables preceding a geminate are counted as closed.

/ɯ/ is realized as [ɯ̟~ɨ]. It is slightly rhotacized before a pause.
/a/ is realized as [ä] following hard consonants, as [a] following soft consonants, and as [æ] before /ɹ/.

Two glides cannot cooccur adjacently within the same syllable. The latter is elided, e.g. lail wayw → way.
y is never pronounced when adjacent to i ü, and w ẅ are never pronounced when adjacent to u ü, unless the vowel is first and in a separate syllable, e.g. itma iyma → ima, kupna kuwna → kuna, but kire chiye. This means ri rü lu are pronounced as i ü u.
After vowels, i ü u are romanized yi ẅü wu and pronounced /ji ɥy ɰɯ/, e.g. iiri ayi /aji/, ellu ewu /ɛɰɯ/.

Word boundaries typically block palatalization, excluding certain set phrases or collocations; this also affects romanization, e.g. läl üta lal üta.

2.4 Stress and phonotactics

Word stress is final if the syllable is closed, otherwise penultimate if the syllable is closed, otherwise antepenultimate. Every other preceding syllable receives secondary stress. In case of exceptions, the stress is marked with an acute in the romanization. The exceptions to the stress rule include:

  • - compound SDOAUX, which are always stressed on the final syllable, e.g. arärä ayayá
  • - second person verb forms ending in -kiu, which are always stressed on the penult, e.g. elikiu elíchu
  • - adjectives and nouns involved in NSWEO agreement, e.g. bee olee vi olí, lika liboa liká livwá
  • - certain short compounds, where stress can be counted as if two words were used, e.g. läl'üta lalǘta (from läl üta lal üta)
  • - certain clipped compounds, e.g. kada kadá (from ka darn ka darn)

The maximum syllable shape is CVC, but:

  • - word-initially sP is allowed
  • - word-medially WsP is allowed
  • - word-finally sP WP Ws WsP LN are allowed

Any instances of word-initial or word-final sb sd sg are respelled as sp st sk, e.g. s- + -dark → stark, pag + -s- → pask.

2.7 Umlaut

Umlaut involves the mutation of a vowel or a vowel sequence when it is followed by an appended front high vowel. This can occur when an inflectional or derivational affix is attached to a word, and in SDOAUX (see 4.4 Clause-final auxiliaries). It does not apply between morphemes in compounds or across word boundaries, so articles, demonstratives and adjectives do not trigger umlaut.

An affixed i changes the preceding vowel or combination thereof - ä a e o u → e ä i e i, e.g. so- GEN + mika thing → semika of thing (GEN-thing).
An affixed ü changes the preceding vowel or combination thereof - ä a e o u → e ä ü ü ü, e.g. does feel + -ü GER → düüsü feeling (feel-GER).
Umlaut does not apply when there is no intervening consonant, e.g. rättä teach + i GER → rättäi teaching (teach-GER).

3 Orthography


3.1 Overview

The script is a semi-syllabary written vertically, with columns read from left to right. The principal feature of the script is a vertical line going through the middle of each column. To the left of the line, the onset and nucleus are encoded. To the right, the coda and/or the voicing of the onset are encoded. In addition, several characters exist for encoding non-CVC syllables.

The script also features four logographs enconding certain grammatical morphology, and a simple system of punctuation.

3.2 Syllabic characters

(Note that the word syllable used across the following sections refers to orthographic syllables, rather than phonological ones.)

◆ CVC characters

The size of a standard character is 20x20u (units). In each word, a vertical line runs through the middle. To the left of the line, syllabic characters representing the onset and nucleus of each syllable are placed; this includes CV and V glyphs. To the right of the line, the characters encoding codas are placed if necessary. All syllabic characters can be found in Appendix 2: Syllabary.

a kü no
at kün nod

Each character occupies a space of 10x17.5u, leaving 2.5u at the bottom to prevent an overlap with the next character. The only exceptions are characters which transition into the line smoothly at the bottom, such as the following.

ü ki su

The right side of the line can also encode voicing of the onset. There are no separate characters for pairs such as ka and ga. Instead, voicing is encoded with a glyph placed to the right of the line, following the voiceless initial glyph. Compare the following:

ka ga te de

Because the coda glyphs and the voicing glyph occupy the same space, each coda has two versions - one which encodes the coda alone, and one which encodes both the coda and the voicing of the onset. Compare the following:

ko go kos gos

When a syllable with an empty right side is followed by a V syllable (but not VC), the latter is mirrored and placed to the right of the former, rather than underneath it. Compare the following:

seon seo dau tau

All examples presented thus far have described characters as used word-internally. However, word-initially and word-finally, the vertical line does not extend throughout the entire height of the character, but the excess is cut off. The points at which the characters connect to the line are hereinafter referred to as vertices. The line begins at the highest vertex of the initial character, and ends at the lowest vertex of the final character.

mikoso adabois eusat

The only exceptions to this rule are the syllabic character pa and the voicing glyph. The vertical line does not connect the two vertices of either character. When either of the characters is combined with one on the other side of the line, the latter connects to the vertical line, which then merges with the higher vertex of the former, unless it is a B character which transitions into the line smoothly at the bottom.

panpapas dagiba
◆ Non-CVC characters

The glyphs described in the previous sections are sufficient when encoding CVC syllables. For more complex syllables, additional characters are used. Interconsonantal s is encoded with a separate 20x20u glyph. Word-initial clustering s and word-final clustering consonants are encoded using 30x10u glyphs, though these glyphs do not need to occupy the entire available space.

stag danska mirn inst
◆ Monogrammic words

Words contained within the height of a single character (20u) are problematic in that the same glyph simultaneously begins and ends the word. To resolve this, several strategies are employed. Each glyph is categorized based on the manner in which it connnects to the vertical line. It can either connect at the top (T), at the bottom (B), in the middle (M), or throughout the length of the line (W). The glyph pa and the voicing glyph are exceptions and will be described separately. These categories can be found in Appendix 2: Syllabary. Depending on this categorization, different strategies are employed when encoding monogrammic words.

If the word contains a character on both sides of the line, and one constituent is T while the other is B, the two are connected by the vertical line between their respective vertices.

rot kän kus

When the pa glyph or the voicing glyph is used in a monogrammic word, its form is identical to the one used word-initially.

gä pa bam pam pak bas

In other cases of combining two characters, the line starts at the highest vertex of the pair, and extends below the character, ending in a 5x5u left-facing loop.

gir pik mis ar

When only a left-side character is used, right-facing loops are used. For B characters, the line extends up from the vertex and ends in a loop at the top of the character box. For T and M characters, the line extends down from the vertex and ends in a loop at the bottom of the character box. For W characters, the line extends down from the highest vertex and ends in a loop directly below the character box.

ki nü ä mi ti su
◆ Syllabification

Syllable breaks avoid coda glyphs followed by vowel glyphs regardless of etymology. For instance, while peom is syllabified as pe-om, peomu is syllabified as pe-o-mu, not pe-om-u. Several compounds break this rule, which is marked in the transliteration with an apostrophe, e.g. läl'üta (syllabified as läl-ü-ta rather than lä-lü-ta). See the examples below.

4 Overarching structures


4.1 Class

Verbs, adjectives and nouns are divided into one of five classes named after cardinal directions; a system referred to as NSWEO. This is similar to grammatical gender, though gender is typically associated with agreement and intrinsic categorization, while this system involves harmony and semantic associations. Combinations of words within the same class are considered semantically stronger and may be used in idioms and collocations. Adjective endings are changed to rhyme with that of the noun if they have the same class. The only form of class agreement is seen in demonstratives and articles.

There are third person pronouns for each of the five classes, though animate referents are not bound to a single class. The classes rather suggest certain features or traits, so the same referent may switch class depending on the context determined by the judgement of the speaker, e.g. a person seen as good by the speaker may be S, while someone perceived as an introvert may be N. Inanimate referents typically stay within one class, though the class may be changed for metaphoric reasons. Each class has a number of possible endings.

CLASS NAME EXAMPLES OF ASSOCIATIONS ENDINGS
North (N) pil piw cold, dark, bad, calm, quiet, still, weak L LP Ls LN LsP
South (S) rio yo hot, bright, good, lively, loud, move, strong B e o
West (W) abi avi masculine, end, old, death, short, hear P ä i
East (E) mon moũ feminine, start, new, young, life, long, say N NP Ns NsP u
Neutral (O) püta püta human, nature s sP ü a

There are a few grammatical words belonging to the O class despite their endings, including gä this, lä what and pronouns ending in either of the two. Several indefinite pronouns which use the negative article as an enclitic retain their class, ignoring the ending of the article, e.g. kusan is O, not E.

There are several clipped compounds which retain their class despite clipping, for example kuska same person (S because it is a clipped form of kus kago).

4.2 Number

Nouns, as well as first and second person pronouns inflect for number using an animacy-based system. The language employs inverse number, meaning each animacy class has an "expected" number which is unmarked and an "unexpected" number which is marked identically across all animacy classes (via reduplication). These classes are independent of NSWEO and each features a different binary number distinction.

  • - set nouns, which include nouns which typically occur in a specific number, e.g. seasons (4), eyes (2), parents (2), the sun (1). Set nouns are unmarked when occurring in their "set" number and marked when occurring in any other number.
  • - animate nouns, which include people and animals. They are unmarked when singular and marked when plural.
  • - inanimate nouns, which include all other nouns. They are unmarked when collective and marked when singulative.

The exact function of the collective-singulative system differs depending on the type of the noun.

  • - count nouns are ambiguous to number and the singulative is applied only to explicitly specify one of something*, e.g. leüt tree(s) → leüleüt a (usually specific) tree
  • - mass nouns are collective by default and singulative when spefifying a unit, e.g. nalul water → nalulul a glass/bottle/unit of water
  • - abstract terms are collective when referring to them as concepts and singulative when referring to specific instances of them, e.g. saekuik murder → Arar saekuguik ari. There's been a murder., kaio love → Äbet kaioio diibi misan usa läläb. Our love will never die.

*In practice this is rarely applied, since such cases are often marked with demonstratives. The singualtive would be generally only used to resolve potential ambiguities.

4.3 Case

There are three cases - direct (nominative + accusative: marks subjects and direct objects), genitive (marks possessors, doubles as ablative) and dative (marks indirect objects, doubles as locative). The genitive and dative cases are also used in conjunction with certain prepositions. The cases may be applied to nouns, pronouns and adjectives, the latter agreeing in case with the noun they describe. However, case prefixes are also applied to verbs to form a number of tenses - the genitive conveys the meaning of past, while the dative conveys the meaning of future/irrealis.

The direct case is unmarked, while genitive and dative marking depends on the animacy of the noun. Animate nouns, non-set personal pronouns, as well as all verbs are marked with one set of prefixes, inanimate nouns are marked with another, and set nouns and pronouns are marked with yet another set. Attributive adjectives take the same set of prefixes as the nouns they describe, cf. semika solo of good thing (GEN-thing GEN-good) and nikus rolo of good person (GEN-person GEN-good). Gerunds take the inanimate prefixes when marked for case, since the animate prefixes are used to indicate tense, e.g. peomu doing (do-GER) → äbpeomu going to do (gerund of future form) (AN.DAT-do-GER), lipeomu at doing (dative of gerund) (INAN.DAT-do-GER).

4.4 Clause-final auxiliaries

Each clause ends with an auxiliary word which encodes the clause's subject and direct object (if there is one), in that order. This is referred to as SDOAUX (Subject Direct Object AUXiliary) and is done using personal pronouns in specific forms. Each pronoun has a default form, which is used in isolation or in SDOAUX in clauses without a direct object. Each pronoun also has a prefixing subject form and a suffixing direct object form. If the subject or direct object in the clause is a pronoun, it only appears in SDOAUX, but if it is a noun, it appears inside the clause in the direct case and is reinforced in SDOAUX using a corresponding pronoun. See 7 Personal pronouns and Appendix 1: SDOAUX.

  • Pask nä. I go. (go-1P 1SG.)
  • Saesk nälu. I kill you. (go-1P 1SG-2SG.)
  • Saek ekki arän. The man kills me. (kill-3P man 3WUM-1SG.)
  • Saesk ekki närä. I kill the man. (kill-1P man 1SG-3WUM.)
  • Saek momk an mion. The woman kills the child. (kill-3P woman child 3EUM-3EUM.)
  • Saek an momk mion. The child kills the woman. (kill-3P child woman 3EUM-3EUM.)

Because both subjects and direct objects take the direct case, some ambiguities may arise and must be resolved through context, e.g. Saesaek kus papa. (kill-REDUP-3P person 3OUM-3OUM.) can mean He killed the person., or The person killed him.

SDOAUX are omitted when the verb in the clause is a copula or a locative copula and the subject is a first or second person pronoun, since these verbs conjugate for number as well as for person. The same is true for SDOAUX which repeat in subsequent clauses - only the final one must be used, provided that the pronouns apply to the same referents.

  • Seon limi. I am here. (LOC.COP-1SG DAT-here.)
  • Ar limi pa. It is here. (LOC.COP-3P DAT-here 3OUM.)
  • Ägä karst, mun nä. I lose, but I learn. (but lose-1P, learn-1P 1SG.)
  • Orsaesk momk nion, saesk an nion. If I kill the woman, I kill the child. (GEN-kill-1SG woman 1SG-3EUM, kill-1SG child 1SG-3EUM.)

There are cases of multiple subjects or direct objects in a clause (x and y). If pronouns are included therein, they are not omitted in favor of SDOAUX. The SDOAUX in such cases always takes the plural (1P and 2P) or marked (3P) form, with the NSWEO determined by the first noun mentioned.

  • Äskest ä nä, kiulu si. You and me are going to die. (DAT-die-1P and 1SG, 2SG 1PL.)
  • Ellikiu ä kiulu, ekki la. You and the man know. (know-2P and 2SG, man 2PL.)
  • Eli äb ekki, momk e leüt, ugu ariri. The man and the woman see the tree and the house. (see-3P and man, woman and tree, house 3WM-3WM.)

Some syncretism within compound SDOAUX occurs, which must be disambiguated through context or spelling, e.g. 3OM-3OUM pab- + -pa → pabpa pappá, 3OM-3OM pab- + -ba → pabba pappá.

5 Articles


5.1 Definite article

The language has a definite article, but no indefinite article. It stems from the demonstrative gä this which is devoiced to kä and further modified based on noun class:

  • - through dissimilation, kä becomes ka after front vowels and class-specific endings are appended
  • - the form is leveled to the S form ko after front vowels, and after back vowels, to the W form ki except the E form kän
  • - an epenthetic neutral vowel a is added at the beginning of the article after consonants except ũ; the rest of the article is clipped

The resultant forms are as follows:

POST-FRONT POST-BACK POST-C (except ũ)
NSWO ko ko ki chi a a
E ko ko kän chaũ a a

If the preceding word has no class (definite articles may appear directly after negative articles), the NSWO form is used.

The vowel-initial article is treated as an enclitic, which is reflected in the pronunciation, e.g. sir a hin a.

The definite article is placed directly after the head noun, before descriptors such as adjectives. It does not inflect for number. The article is not used as commonly as it is in English. It is primarily used:

  • - with set nouns and proper nouns or noun phrases, e.g. nid a the sun, bis a the world
  • - with question clauses, e.g. Piellin lilä ko sonläluguu nipa näpa. I don't know where he lives. (NEG-know-1P where DEF.ART GEN-live-GER GEN-3OUM 1SG-3OUM.)
  • - in place of possessive pronouns or demonstratives, particularly when they have been previously mentioned or are obvious from context, e.g. Räbeeb'n lisurs a mirn nark. I hurt the (my) head. (give-REDUP-1P DAT-head DEF.ART pain 1SG-3NUM.)
  • - when referring to a specific referent that has previously been mentioned, e.g. Elilin ugu nion. Ar do ugu kän mion. I saw a house. The house was big. (see-REDUP-1P house 1SG-3EUM. COP-REDUP-3P big house DEF.ART 3EUM.)
  • - when referring to a specific referent and all interlocutors are assumed to know what is being referred to, e.g. Edest mikaäbbaba ki näpa. I finished the task (that we all know of). (end-REDUP-1P task DEF.ART 1SG-3OUM.)
  • - after head nouns of subordinate clauses, e.g. kus a selii rän the person who sees me (person DEF.ART GEN-see-GER GEN-1SG)
  • - for superlatives when used after adjectives, cf. ugu kän do the big house (house DEF.ART big), ugu do ki the biggest house (house big DEF.ART)
  • - for ordinal numbers, e.g. pik a third (3 DEF.ART)
5.2 Negative article

The negative article is indefinite and is the equivalent of the English no ~. It stems from the abbreviated gerund of the negative locative copula nü(r) → nü, essentially meaning lacking. For the most part, its forms mirror the patterns of the definite article:

POST-FRONT POST-BACK POST-C
NSWO no no nüi nẅi an aũ
E no no nüän nẅaũ an aũ

The negative article is used:

  • - to negate indefinite sentences, that is those in which the direct object is not a pronoun, or a noun followed by a definite article, demonstrative or possessive pronoun, cf. Pielin ugu kän nion. I don't see the house. (NEG-see-1P house DEF.ART 1SG-3EUM.) and Elin ugu nüän nion. I don't see a house. (see-1P house NEG.ART 1SG-3EUM.).
  • - to form certain indefinite pronouns, e.g. mikan nothing (see 10.1 Negative article - none/some)
  • - to negate adjectives, when placed after them instead of after the noun, cf. ugu nüän do no big house (house NEG.ART big) and ugu do nüi not-big house (house big NEG.ART)

The negative article is used with unmarked nouns, with two excpetions:

  • - with set nouns with the set number of one, e.g. Lä kiti badans liuliu nüän mionon. No life is too short. (COP-3P short too life-REDUP NEG.ART 3EM.)
  • - with abstract inanimate nouns used as instances, e.g. Saesaek mimirn an misda tadän. No pain has killed me yet. (kill-REDUP-3P pain-REDUP NEG.ART still 3NM-1SG.)

6 Nouns


6.1 Overview

Nouns inflect for case, with the prefixes chosen based on the noun's animacy (see 4.3 Case). They inflect for number, which is also based on the noun's animacy (see 4.2 Number). Polysemous nouns may have different animacy for each meaning, e.g. püta nature - inanimate, manner, being like - set (1). This must be taken into account when inflecting nouns, cf. spüta (GEN-nature) and diüta (GEN-being.like).

There are nouns whose animacy may not be obvious. Set nouns may be animate or inanimate, but they will always belong to the set class. It can be said that the set class takes priority over the other two. There are several nouns which do not refer to humans or animals, but are animate nonetheless, e.g. usa heart, soul, self, identity.

Nouns may be derived from other parts of speech through affixation, e.g. danska speech (from dansk *say + -a NOM), but the language features zero-derivation as well, so certain words may be both nouns and adjectives, e.g. ekki man, masculine, or both nouns and verbs, e.g. liu life, live. For more information, see 17 Word formation.

Nouns belong to one of five NSWEO classes, which is reflected in the noun's ending. This means that compounds whose head noun determines the NSWEO are often split to preserve the ending, e.g. et end + usa self → eusat death. For more information, see 17 Word formation.

Except for the words ekki man, male and momk woman, female, nouns are not sex-specific. To specify the sex of a noun the aformenetioned two words can be used attributively, e.g. an child, boy, girl → an ekki boy, an momk girl.

7 Personal pronouns


7.1 First and second person pronouns

Since first and second person pronouns practically never have inanimate referents, there is only a distinction between singular, plural and set forms. Set pronouns are used when referring to a specific, countable group of entities, e.g. a couple, a team, a herd, a council. If an inanimate object is referred to in first or second person, it is assumed to be personified and is treated as animate.

Singular pronouns are unmarked, while the others are derived from the singular prefixing forms with the word bis all marking set pronouns and boa many marking plural pronouns. The first person plural pronoun stems from a separate root.

Each pronoun has a base form used in isolation, a prefixing subject form and a suffixing direct object form, the latter two used in compound SDOAUX. The oblique genitive and dative forms are created by attaching case prefixes to the suffixing forms of the pronouns. 7.3 Possessives

The genitive pronouns double as possessive pronouns. Similarly to articles and demostratives, possessive pronouns are placed directly after the head noun, before other descriptors, e.g. skaian rän olo my good friend (friend GEN-1SG good).

When the possessor is the subject of the sentence, personal possessive pronouns are usually replaced with either the definite article or the reflexive pronoun. In third person, genitive pronouns can be used to distinguish between one's own and someone else's possession.

  • Doesoens ümpüi ko mienarn nark. I felt the rain in the (my) eyes. (feel-REDUP-1P DAT-eye DEF.ART rain 1SG-1NUM.)
  • Paba lugu rusa pa. He went to his (own) house. (go-REDUP-3P DAT-house GEN-REFL 3OUM.)
  • Paba lugu nipa pa. He went to his (someone else's) house. (go-REDUP-3P DAT-house GEN-3OUM 3OUM.)

Genitive pronouns can only function as possessive determinants. To use possessive pronouns, the impersonal demonstrative gä is appended before the genitive pronoun, e.g. Lä gä orlu an gän mion. This child is yours. (COP-3P DEM GEN-2SG child DEM 3EUM.).

7.4 Reflective pronouns

The reflexive pronoun usa usa stems from the word usa self, heart, identity and has no SDOAUX forms. When it is used as the direct object, the SDOAUX only features the subject form (see 14.8 Impersonal and reflexive expressions).

The genitive form of the reflexive pronoun is rusa nusa, while the dative form is äbsa assa (note the irregular pronunciation).

8 Interrogatives


8.1 lä, kuä - what, who

The basic interrogative lä la means what and other interrogatives are derived from it. Despite its ending, lä and compounds ending in the word belong to the neutral class. It is classed as inanimate. It does not inflect for number and always uses unmarked pronouns. Unlike in English, the word cannot be used attributively; for this purpose, the word sonlä is used (see 8.2 sonlä, lilä - which, whence, where, whither).

  • Dapeom an lä mipa? What is the child doing? (INT-do-3P child what 3EUM-3OUM?)
  • Piellin lä ko situns skä spa. We don't know what started this. (NEG-know-1P what DEF.ART GEN-start-GER GEN-DEM 1PL-3OUM.)

The word kuä kwa means who and is derived from kus lä what person. Like lä, it is classed as neutral and does not inflect for number, taking only unmarked pronouns. Unlike lä, it is animate. The genitive is orkuä orkwa whose, of whom, from whom, and the dative is äbkuä akkwa to whom.

  • Dar püta skä kuä pa? Who said this? (INT-COP-REDUP-3P like GEN-DEM who 3OUM?)
  • Dadaäbeel äbkuä kiulu? Who are you talking to? (INT-talk-2P DAT-who 2SG?)
  • Dadaäbeel orkuä kiulu? Who are you talking about? (INT-talk-2P GEN-who 2SG?)
  • Dalä gä orkuä ugu gän mion? Whose is this house? (INT-COP-3P DEM GEN-who house DEM 3EUM?)
8.2 sonlä, lilä - which, whence, where, whither

The words sonlä sownya and lilä lila have various meanings. Firstly, they can be used as the genitive and dative forms of lä what. Secondly, they can refer to location, meaning whence (from where) and where (at what place), whither (to what place), respectively. Both words are inanimate.

  • Dapabal limimi rot sonlä kiulu? With what (mode of transportation) did you go there? (INT-go-REDUP-2P DAT-there through GEN-what 2SG?)
  • Dar kaian lilä äblu mion? What do you like? (INT-LOC.COP-3P fondness DAT-what DAT-2SG 3EUM?)
  • Danänälk sonlä kiulu? Where do you come from? (INT-come-REDUP-2P whence 2SG?)
  • Däbseon lidoreo seniko lilä? Where can I find happiness? (INT-DAT-LOC.COP-1SG DAT-find-GER GEN-happiness where?)
  • Dar ugu orlu lilä mion? Where is your house? (INT-LOC.COP-3P house GEN-2SG where 3EUM?)

The other usage of sonlä is after nouns, in which case it means which ~, what ~, what kind of ~. Similarly to possessives, it can only be used attributively, and to use it nominally, the demonstrative gä must precede it. In this sense, it could be used as the English how.

  • Danänälk stü sonlä kiulu? What place do you come from? (INT-come-REDUP-2P GEN-place which 2SG?)
  • Dalä ugu orlu gä sonlä pa? Which (one) is your house? (INT-COP-3P house GEN-2SG DEM which 3OUM?)
  • Dalä gä sonlä ugu orlu mion? How is your house? (INT-COP-3P DEM which house GEN-2SG 3EUM?)
8.3 imi, baläns, boalä - when, why, how, how many, how much

The word imi imi means when and is derived from limis lä at what time. It is inanimate and belongs to the neutral class.

  • Dedet imi usa pa? When did he die? (INT-end-REDUP-3P when self 3OUM?)
  • Lälä mikan rütnü ellii simi ko säbnämisdal ris. I want to know when we will come back. (COP-1SG nothing without know-GER GEN-when DEF.ART GEN-DAT-return-GER GEN-1PL.)

The word baläns valaũs means both why and how, and is derived from bans lä for what. It is inanimate and belongs to the neutral class. In case of ambiguity, why can be expressed as bans koütinst sonlä for what reason and how can be expressed as rot sorot sonlä by what way.

  • Dedet balans usa pa? How did he die? (INT-end-REDUP-3P how self 3OUM?)
  • Däbdosuesiikiu balans kiulu? Why do you have to leave? (INT-DAT-leave-REDUP-2P why 2SG?)

The word boalä vwala means how many, how much and is derived from boa lä what many. It is inanimate and belongs to the neutral class. It can be used attributively and nominally. It can also be used to quantify adjectives and adverbs when placed after them.

  • Dar anan boalä äblu mionon? How many children do you have? (INT-LOC.COP-3P child-REDUP how.many DAT-2SG 3EM?)
  • Dellikiu boalä ko storeo nipa kipa? Do you know how much he won? (INT-know-2P how.much DEF.ART GEN-win-GER GEN-3OUM 2SG-3OUM?)
  • Dalä nantee boalä stadag go läläb? How strong is this fire? (INT-COP-3P strong how.much fire-REDUP DEM 3SM?)

9 Demonstratives


9.1 Basic demonstrative

The basic demonstrative stems from the word gä ja, which, like lä, belongs to the neutral class despite its ending. This demostrative can be used attributively in which case it acts much like the definite article, agreeing with the NSWEO of the head noun and being placed directly after it, before any descriptors, e.g. ekki gi bog this tall man. It does not inflect for number or case.

The base form gä is used for the neutral class; for other NSWEO, class-specific endings are appended.

NORTH gäl jaw
SOUTH go go
WEST gi ji
EAST gän jaũ
NEUTRAL gä ja

The neutral gä can also be used as a demonstrative pronoun, being the equivalent of English this. It can be used in impersonal constructions, acting similarly to the English it and one (see 14.8 Impersonal and reflexive expressions). In some cases, gä is practically interchangeable with the neutral pronoun pa.

  • Lä ugu gä pa. This is a house. (COP-3P house DEM 3OUM.)
  • Lä ugu pa. It is a house. (COP-3P house 3OUM.)
9.2 Proximal and distal demonstratives

Except for the words mi here and mimi there (see 9.3 Other forms), there is no distinction between proximal and distal demonstratives. However, the adjectives müi mẅi near and dans daũs far may be placed directly before the head to convey proximity (in this context müi and dans do not participate in adjective agreement).

  • Lä do müi ugu gän mion. This house is big. (COP-3P big near house DEM 3EUM.)
  • Saesaesk dans momk gän nion. I killed that woman. (kill-REDUP-1P far woman DEM 1SG-3EUM.)
  • Piiliubamt müi bamt skä mion. This kind of flower doesn't blossom. (NEG-blossom-3P near flower such 3EUM.)
  • Orpeomk dans mins kipa. Don't do it like that. (GEN-do-2P far thus 2SG-3OUM.)
9.3 Other forms - skä, eligä, boagä, mins, mi, mimi

Since gä is the demonstrative equivalent of lä, several demostratives can be derived from interrogatives by replacing the latter with the former. However, the meanings of the resultant forms are not necessarily predictable from the interrogatives.

The form skä hcha is the genitive form of the demonstrative gä, and is also used to mean such, this kind of. Like its interrogative equivalent sonlä, it can only be used attributively, and gä must precede it when used nominally. The form eligä elija is the dative form of gä (note the irregular case marking), and is also used to mean then, at that time, and. This word can be further inflected into the genitive seligä hyelija from then and the dative leligä lelija till then. Finally, boagä vwaja is the demonstrative equivalent of boalä, and means this much, this many.

  • Ellin dü skä näpa. I know its place. (know-1P place GEN-DEM 1SG-3OUM.)
  • Ebnüä lipeomu semikaäbbaba skä. I can't do such tasks. (DAT-NEG.LOC.COP-1SG DAT-do-GER GEN-task such.)
  • Ar kaian eligä äb'n mion. I like this. (LOC.COP-3P fondness DAT-DEM DAT-1SG 3EUM.)
  • Rot säikkiu eligä an kiion. You should kill the child then. (by kill-2P then child 2SG-3EUM.)
  • Lälä mikan rütnü kaio boagä. I want this much love. (COP-1SG nothing without love this.much.)
  • Lä do boagä leüt mion. The tree is this big. (COP-3P big this.much tree 3EUM.)

The word mins myeũs means thus, like this, so and is derived from bans mi for here. Apart from its demonstrative use, it is also the word meaning yes.

  • Lä nantee mins stadag a läläb. The fire is so strong. (COP-3P strong so fire-REDUP DEF.ART 3SM.)
  • Dadaäbeel mins baläns kiulu? Why are you talking like this? (INT-talk-2P thus why 2SG.)
  • Mins, rättädä mins pän. Yes, he taught me thus. (yes, teach-REDUP-3P thus 3OUM-1SG.)

The word mi mi means here and is derived from müi close. Its reduplicated form mimi mimi means there. Both words act only as nouns, and to use them adverbially, case marking must be used. The genitive forms semi hyemi and semimi hyemimi mean hence (from here) and thence (from there), respectively. The dative forms limi limi and limimi limimi mean here (at this place), hither (to this place) and there (at that place), thither (to that place), respectively.

  • Lä dü olo mi arä. It's a good place here. (COP-3P place good here 3WUM.)
  • Äbpabask semi si. We need to get out of here. (DAT-go-REDUP-1P GEN-here 1PL.)
  • Piäbpa momk limimi mion. The woman will not go there. (NEG-DAT-go-3P woman DAT-there 3EUM.)
  • Ar nid a limimi läb. The sun is there. (LOC.COP-3P sun DEF.ART DAT-there 3SUM.)

10 Indefinite pronouns and adjectives


10.1 Negative article - none/some

Negative pronouns are formed with the negative article. The article by itself translates to English no ~ and can be used to negate indefinite sentences (see 5.2 Negative article). Verbs are not negated when used with negative articles in their original meaning. Negating the verb reverses the meaning of the article to some ~, since a phrase like Piipeoms mikan näpa. lit. I don't do nothing. is interpreted as It is not true that I do nothing. = I do something.

  • Lä do mins ugu nüän mion. No house is that big. (COP-3P big thus house NEG.ART 3OUM.)
  • Saesaesk momk an nänon. I didn't kill any woman. (kill-REDUP-1P woman NEG.ART 1SG-3EM.)
  • Piidaäbeen äbpa rekki no nä. I talk to him about some man. (NEG-talk-1P DAT-3OUM GEN-man NEG.ART 1SG.)
  • Piidaäbeen äbekki no nipa nä. I talk to some man about him. (NEG-talk-1P DAT-man NEG.ART GEN-3OUM 1SG.)

  • Piinänän sünür nä. I came from somewhere. (NEG-come-REDUP-1P GEN-NEG.LOC.COP-GER 1SG.)
  • Ar üta semikan arä. The man said nothing. (COP-REDUP-3P like GEN-nothing 3WUM.)
  • Piidaäbeen äbkusan semikan nä. I talk to someone about something. (NEG-talk-1P DAT-nobody GEN-nothing 1SG.)
  • Ar mikan äb'n pa. I have nothing. (LOC.COP-3P nothing DAT-1SG 3OUM.)
  • Nü ligir nikusan pa. He was fighting someone. (NEG.LOC.COP-3P DAT-fight-GER GEN-nobody 3OUM.)
10.2 bis - every

The word bis vis has various uses and meanings. It can be used as a set noun to mean the world or to convey spatial relations, with the meaning of within, among, inside. It can be used as an adjective to mean all, every, each, or as a pronoun to mean everything, everyone. For disambiguation, the adjectival phrases mika bisika mika vihíka every thing and kus bisus kus visus every person can be used.

Other than that, bis is used as an enclitic with two pronouns and two nouns.

10.3 Potential form - any

In most cases in which English would use pronouns such as anything or whatever, the pronoun is synonymous with some or every, e.g. Is anyone (someone) interested?, I can do anything (everything). Therefore, there is no need for such pronouns in this language. However, in subjunctive clauses, a plain interrogative is used and the predicate is conjugated into the potential form (see 15.7 Potential).

  • Äbseon lidiii sugu sonlä, äbarar kaian ebion äb'n mion. Whichever house I choose, I'll like it. (DAT-LOC.COP-1SG DAT-choose-GER GEN-house which, DAT-LOC.COP-3P fondness DAT-3EUM DAT-1SG 3EUM.)

  • Äbar kuä linäl äb'n pa, lälä mikan rütnü uloo nipa. Whoever comes to me, I want to hug them. (DAT-LOC.COP-3P who DAT-come-GER DAT-1SG 3OUM, COP-1SG nothing without hug-GER GEN-3OUM.)

11 Adjectives and adverbs


11.1 Placement and characteristics of adjectives

Since the language is exclusively head-initial, adjectives always follow nouns they modify. If an article, demonstrative, or possessive pronoun is used, the adjective follows it. This does not apply to nouns used in the genitive case to express possession, cf. ugu olo rekki the man's good house (house good GEN-man) and ugu rekki rolo the good man's house (house GEN-man GEN-good). Adjectives cannot be used attributively with pronouns.

Adjectives are noun-like and have no predicative forms. They are used with the copula, similarly to nouns. Adjectives used predicatively do not participate in ending agreement (see 11.2 Agreement of adjectives with nouns) or number agreement.

  • Lä bee olee läb. It's good light. (COP-3P light good 3SUM.)
  • Lä olo bee läb. Light is good. (COP-3P good light 3SUM.)
  • Lä nantee ekki arä. The man is strong. (COP-3P strong man 3WUM.)
  • Lä nantee ekkigi ari. Men are strong. (COP-3P strong man-REDUP 3WM.)

Except for certain constructions, adjectives cannot be used nominally. Nominalizing them can be done either by using a generic noun like kus person or mika thing, or by using the demonstrative, e.g. gä bilp the weak, one who is weak, those who are weak.

Adejctives have a negative form used in certain constructions described in further sections (see 11.6 Comparison of adjectives and adverbs). The form is created by attatching the negative article directly after the adjective. This is possible because the negative article otherwise directly follows the head noun.

  • bee no olee no good light (light NEG.ART good)
  • bee olee no not good light (light good NEG.ART)
11.2 Agreement of adjectives with nouns

Adjectives agree with the nouns they modify in case and number. Case prefixes are applied in agreement with the animacy of the noun.

  • mika olo good thing(s), kus olo good person
  • mikaga ololo a good thing, kugus ololo good people
  • semika solo of good thing(s), nikus rolo of good person
  • limikaga lololo to a good thing, äbkugus äbololo to good people

Contrary to number and case, adjectives do not necessarily have to agree with the noun's class, since each adjective belongs to one of NSWEO. If an adjective does have the same class as the noun it modifies, its ending changes to rhyme with the noun. The appended ending starts from the stressed vowel of the noun, except for nouns with antepenultimate stress whose ending starts with the penultimate vowel, e.g. eus-at, n-oba, mik-oso. Preceding semivowels are included provided they stem from orthographic vowels, e.g. l-iu, ka-ian, but nal-ul.

For consonant-final adjectives, the ending is appended to the end of the adjective. For vowel-final adjectives it replaces the final vowel(s), unless the adjective is monosyllabic in which case an intervening epenthetic r (before front vowels) or l (before back vowels) is placed between the adjective and the ending. Finally, the adjective must be stressed on the same syllable as the noun.

  • mienarn + bilp → mienarn bilparn myenarn viwparn weak rain
  • mika + bis → mika bisika mika vihíka everything
  • nid + kago → nid kagid nid kajid same sun
  • kaio + nantee → kaio nantaio kayo naũtáyo strong love
  • mikoso + olo → mikoso oloso mikoso owoso good truth
  • kus + boa → kus boalus kus vwawus many people
  • ekki + müi → ekki müirekki ecchi mẅiyecchi distant man

When nouns are reduplicated, the agreeing adjectives are reduplicated and then have their endings changed, e.g. ekki + müi → ekkigi müimüi → ekkigi müimigi ecchiji mẅimiji distant men. Potential case prefixes are appended using these forms as the base.

When a noun agreeing with an adjective is inflected, the adjective inflects regularly, using its rhyming direct form as the base, e.g. ka boa ka vwa many sides → lika liboa liká livwá, not *lika liboarika lika livwayíka. Moreover, pairs of agreeing nouns and adjectives have fixed stress when case prefixes are applied, e.g. ka bisa ka visá every side → lika libisa liká livisá.

11.3 Placement and characteristics of adverbs

Adverbs do not have NSWEO and exhibit no agreement. Similarly to adjectives, adverbs follow the word they modify. Adverbs can modify adjectives, verbs, or other adverbs. When an adverb modifies a verb, it generally does not directly follow it, unless it is emphasized, because usually the verb is directly followed by the subject.

  • Lä bilp roba beebee ko läläb. The light is very weak. (COP-3P weak very light-REDUP DEF.ART 3SM.)
  • Mu an rot olo mion. The child is learning well. (learn-3P child by good 3EUM.)
  • Stagbee stadag a rot nantee roba läläb. The fire is burning very strongly. (burn-3P fire-REDUP DEF.ART by strong very 3SM.)

Adverbials are typically placed between the subject and the direct object. Although sentences are usually verb-initial, adverbial phrases are sometimes placed at the beginning of a sentence, especially if they are long and introducing them first is desired by the speaker. This fronting can also be done as a form of disambiguation, emphasizing that the adverbial refers to the entire clause, not the verb itself.

  • Eligä misk, äbet rot arä. And now, the path will end. (then now, DAT-end-3P path 3WUM.)
  • Pabask misada lugu nipa nä. Today, I went to the man's house. (go-REDUP-1P today DAT-house GEN-3OUM 1SG.)
  • Rot semirn, edet usa pa. Unfortunately, he died. (through sadness, end-REDUP-3P self 3OUM.)
  • Edet rot semirn usa pa. He died unfortunately. (end-REDUP-3P through sadness self 3OUM.)
11.4 Conversion of adjectives into adverbs

The preposition rot by, through can be placed in front of adjectives to turn them into adverbs, e.g. olo good → rot olo well. While this preposition typically requires the following word to be in the genitive case, there is no case marking if rot is used as an adverbializer.

Adverbs derived from adjectives do not inflect for number or case. However, they can be negated with the negative article, similarly to their adjectival equivalents, e.g. rot olo nüi not well.

11.5 Adverbs of degree

To convey excessiveness, the word badans vadaũs overdo, excessive(ly) can be used as an adverb. To express insufficiency, the word bamüi vamẅi underdo, insufficient(ly) is used. These stem from the combination of the verb ba *do and the adjectives dans far and müi near.

Another strategy is to use augmentatives or diminutives. Augmentatives used with adjectives or adverbs with positive meaning express excessiveness, while diminutives used with adjectives or adverbs with negative meaning express insufficiency. When an adjective or adverb with a positive meaning is diminutivized, it conveys the meaning of slight excessiveness.

  • Lä milk badans ugu mion. The house is too small. (COP-3P small excessively house 3EUM.)
  • Sanskakiu rot olo bamüi kiulu. You are writing not well enough. (write-2P by good insufficiently 2SG.)
  • Lä matamilkan matosa orlu pa. Your voice is too quiet. (COP-3P quiet-DIM voice GEN-2SG 3OUM.)
  • Nänäl kugus rot boado paba. lit. People came too mani-ly. = Too many people came. (come-REDUP-3P person-REDUP by many-AUG 3OM.)
  • Lä bemsil rot arä. The path is a little too long. (COP-3P long-DIM path 3WUM.)

To express sufficiency, the word müidansa mẅidaũsa enough, just right is used. It stems from the adjectives müi near and dans far, and the nominalizer -a (from mika thing).

  • Lä bog müidansa momk mion. The woman is tall enough. (COP-3P tall enough woman 3EUM.)
  • Girk rot olo müidansa kiulu. You are fighting well enough. (fight-2P by good enough 2SG.)

The adverb roba nova very (from rot way + boa many), and the indefinite pronouns retbis yeppis in all ways, completely and rotan notaũ not at all can be used both attributively and predicatively.

  • olo roba very good (good very)
  • Sansk rot olo roba pa. He writes very well. (write-3P by good very 3OUM.)
  • sosar sostagbee retbis completely burnt (GEN-take-GER GEN-burn-GER completely)
  • Lä uso retbis pa. It is completely true. (COP-3P true completely 3OUM.)
  • olo rotan in no way good (good nohow)
  • Lä kago rotan müi mikaga gä sab paba. These two things are not at all the same. (COP-3P same nohow near thing-REDUP DEM two 3OM.)

To express the meaning of quite, rather, the word dar or can be placed after an adjective or adverb. This can be done attributively and predicatively. In the latter case the auxiliary verb ba *do is used and the adjective is used nominally as a quasi-direct object, thus it does not participate in NSWEO agreement with the subject noun. The meaning not very can be achieved by negating the verb, but this form cannot be used attributively.

  • mika do dar quite a big thing (thing big or)
  • Ba mienanarn a matado dar tarläb. The rain is quite loud. (do-3P rain-REDUP DEF.ART loud or 3NUM-3SUM.)
  • Piiba leüt dans dar mion. The tree is not very far. (NEG-do-3P tree far or 3EUM-3EUM.)
11.6 Comparison of adjectives and adverbs

There is no inflection involved with comparison. To express the comparative degree, the noun that is being compared to is used in the dative case and placed after the adjective/adverb. When there is no compared noun, the demonstrative gä is used. The adjective/adverb can be affirmative to express more or negative to express less.

  • ekki do lileüt man bigger than a tree (man big DAT-tree)
  • Lä do nüi bamt lileüt mion. A flower is less big than a tree. (COP-3P big NEG.ART flower DAT-tree 3EUM.)
  • Lälä mikan rütnü elii rot olo eligä. I want to see better. (COP-1SG nothing without see-GER by good DAT-DEM.)

This construction means that the beneficiary of an adjective cannot be expressed with the dative case. Instead, the preposition bans is used, cf. olo äb'n better than me and olo bans nä good to me.

The superlative is formed by placing the definite article directly after the adjective/adverb, whereas normally it would be placed directly after the head noun.

  • Daäbeenkan rot uso ki nä. I spoke the most truthfully. (talk-REDUP-1P by true DEF.ART 1SG.)
  • Lä mika uso an ki imbis a pa. This is the least true thing in the world. (COP-3P thing true NEG.ART DEF.ART DAT-world DEF.ART 3OUM.)

To compare two nouns of equal quality, a similar construction is used as with the comparative degree, but the adjectival/adverbial phrase contains the negative form followed by the affirmative form.

  • Lä bog an bog momk äbekki mion. The woman is as tall as the man. (COP-3P tall NEG.ART tall woman DAT-man 3EUM.)
  • Saesaesk kugus boa nüi boa äbpa näba. I've killed as many people as he has. (kill-REDUP-1P person-REDUP many NEG.ART many DAT-3OUM 1SG-3OM.)
  • Räb danska rot nantee no rot nantee mirn park. Words hurt as strongly as murder. (give-3P word by strong NEG.ART by strong pain 3OUM-3NUM.)

The adjectives boa vwa many and tiü chiẅ few can be used to mean more, the most and fewer/less, the fewest/the least when compared. They can only refer to quantities and cannot be used to compare other adjectives as in English.

  • Arn mikan rütnü gä boa eligä. I wanted more. (COP-REDUP-1SG nothing without DEM many DAT-DEM.)
  • Lemt store kaian boa ki läion. The winner gathers the most love. (gather-3P winner fondness many DEF.ART 3SUM-3EUM.)
  • Ar skaiagaian tiü äbpa äb'n mionon. He has fewer friends than me. (LOC.COP-3P friend-REDUP few DAT-3OUM DAT-1SG 3EM.)

12.2 Ordinal numbers

Ordinals are formed by attaching the definite article directly after the adjectival form of the number, for instance pik a third (3 DEF.ART), e saba diüü, sab a forty-second (and 2-NOM GEN-20, 2 DEF.ART). The only exceptions are sinst first (GEN-start) and soüt second, next (GEN-leading). These exceptions only apply to the numbers one and two, and for insatnce twenty-first is el üü, iis a, not *el üü, sinst.

With the exception of sinst and soüt, ordinals act like adjectival numerals, taking case prefixes which agree in animacy with the head noun and being invariable with respect to number. Because sinst and soüt are genitive by default, they take no additional case marking.

  • äbkus äbmin ko (DAT-person DAT-4 DEF.ART) to the fourth person
  • äbkugus äbmin ko (DAT-person-REDUP DAT-4 DEF.ART) to the fourth person
  • äbkus sinst (DAT-person GEN-start) to the first person
  • äbkugus sinst (DAT-person-REDUP GEN-start) to the first people

The interrogative skä which can be used with the definite article to form skä ko which (in order). The ordinals can be used adverbially as well, for instance rot sinst firstly, rot pik a thirdly, rot set lastly.

Conjunctions/prepositions

Conjunctions and prepositions constitute one lexical category (hereafter referred to as particles). Particles can attach to a verb in certain constructions, e.g. kus a spago-äbee rän lit. the person of my going-with = the person who is going with me (person DEF.ART GEN-go-GER-with GEN-1SG). In place of particles, case marking can be used to indicate the function of a word in a sentence. The genitive shows possession, but also means from, or about. The dative marks indirect objects, but also location and destination. When case prefixes are attached to the ends of verbs, the forms -son and -eli are used invariably.

Tense/aspect system

The verbal paradigm is a modified version of a proto-system in which verbs may be modified in two ways: by applying case (direct - non-past, genitive - past, dative - future/irrealis) and by applying other strategies (plain form - realis, LOC.COP DAT-GER - imperfective, reduplication - irrealis). Three forms are shifted in the current system: conditional → perfect → potential. The old system may be used in certain solidified expressions or constructions. The current system is as follows:

DIRECT GENITIVE DATIVE
PLAIN present conditional future
LOC.COP DAT-GER imperfect past habitual potential
REDUP perfect past potential necessitative

The forms serve the following functions:

PRESENT present progressive or habitual actions - I do, I am doing
IMPERFECT past progressive actions that have either finished in the past or have lasted until the present - I was doing, I have been doing
PERFECT past perfective actions - I did, I have done
CONDITIONAL conditions - if I do, if I had done
PAST HABITUAL habitual actions which occurred in the past - I used to do, I would do
PAST POTENTIAL past actions which may have happened - I may have done, I could have done, maybe I have done
FUTURE future perfective or progressive actions - I will do, I am going to do, I will be doing, I will have done
POTENTIAL capability or possibility - I can do, I could do, I may do, maybe I will do
NECESSITATIVE necessity or obligation - I must do, I have to do

When using constructions with the locative copula, the direct object of the clause is in the genitive case and follows the gerund of the lexical verb directly. This is different from regular sentences in which the direct object is typically clause-final. If the subject in such clauses is a noun, it is placed between the locative copula and the gerund. When the verb has an indirect object and a direct object, the indirect object follows the direct object, acting as an adverbial phrase. However, if the direct object is not stated, case prefixes/particles are attached to the end of the gerund and the object is in the genitive case.

  • Seon lelii. lit. I am at seeing. = I was looking. (LOC.COP-1SG DAT-see-GER.)
  • Seon lelii sugu. lit. I am at looking of a house. = I was looking at a house. (LOC.COP-1SG DAT-see-GER GEN-house.)
  • Ar ekki lelii ormomk arä. lit. The man is at seeing of the woman. = The man was looking at the woman. (LOC.COP-3P DAT-see-GER GEN-woman 3WUM.)
  • Seosk läbeekuns-eli rekki. lit. You are at talking-to of the man. = You were talking to the man. (LOC.COP-2SG DAT-talk-GER-to GEN-man.)
  • Ebsis likusoo spamt äbekki. lit. We to-are at giving of a flower to the man. = We can give a flower to the man. (DAT-LOC.COP-1PL DAT-offer-GER GEN-flower DAT-man.)
  • Orseon lidotun semika sugu. lit. I of-am at taking of things from the house. = I used to take things from the house. (GEN-LOC.COP-1SG DAT-take-GER GEN-thing GEN-house.)

Copulae

There is a copula and a locative copula. The copula is followed directly by its complement (a noun or an adjective). The locative copula expresses existence and is the equivalent of the English to be (somewhere) and there is (something somewhere). It is also used to indicate possession. Rather than using a verb like to have, the locative copula is used, with its complement being the possessee and the possessor being used in the dative, e.g. Ar mis äb'n pa. lit. There is time to me. = I have time. (LOC.COP-3P time DAT-1SG 3OUM.).

The copulae are the only verbs which do not require SDOAUX, unless the subject is in third person. This is because they are also the only verbs to conjugate for person in first and second person. The first person set and plural endings are -bi and -(i)s respectively, and the second person endings are -il and -al; these stem from personal pronouns.

Negation and interrogation

With the exception of the copulae, verbs are negated with a prefix pi- before vowels (p- in case of an illegal vowel sequence) and pii- before consonants; this stems from the verb piir differ. The copula is negated by placing its complement in the genitive case, cf. Lä dü ugu mion. A house is a place. (COP-3P place house 3EUM.) and Lä südü bamt mion. A flower is not a place. (COP-3P GEN-place flower 3EUM). This is because the copula stems from the verb meaning stay and the genitive conveys the meaning of staying (away) from. When the complement is an adjective, the inanimate genitive prefix is used. The locative copula is negated with the verb nür.

Verbs are negated only in definite sentences. Sentences with transitive verbs whose DO is not a pronoun or a noun followed by a definite article, demonstrative or possessive pronoun are negated by attaching the negative article to the DO.

The negative prefix precedes any tense/aspect marking, including the conditional form, e.g. Piorpeomk kipa, äbpeoms näpa. If you don't do it, I will. (NEG-GEN-do-2P 2SG-3OUM, DAT-do-1P 1SG-3OUM.). Gerunds of negated verbs function as nouns and case prefixes are therefore placed before the negative prefix, e.g. kus spielii lit. person of not-seeing = person who doesn't see (person GEN-NEG-see-GER).

Questions are marked with a verbal prefix d- before vowels and da- before consonants; this stems from the verb darn other. The interrogative prefix always precedes the negative prefix, if one is used. The two prefixes always precede tense/aspect prefixes. This prefix is subject to umlaut. The combination of the two in the form däpii can be used as a tag.

Imperatives

Imperatives are formed by using the conditional tense. The negative form is used for affirmative imperatives and the affirmative form is used for negative imperatives. The subject of the clause is the recipient of the imperative; if the SDOAUX is kiulu, it is typically omitted.

  • Ornälk limi (kiulu). lit. If you come here. = Don't come here. (GEN-come-2P DAT-here [2SG].)
  • Piorsaesk spa. lit. If we don't kill him. = Let's kill him. (NEG-GEN-kill-1P 1PL-3OUM.)
  • Ürnü liuliu ololo äbpa mionon. lit. If there isn't a good life for him. = May he have a good life. (GEN-NEG.LOC.COP-3P life-REDUP good-REDUP DAT-3OUM 3EM.)
Passive and causative voice

Passive and causative voice is expressed with the auxiliaries nors take and räbee give respectively. The verbs are followed by gerunds. In passive constructions, the agent is expressed with the genitive as in He takes the woman's killing. = He is killed by a woman., unless the verb takes any arguments, in which case the agent is expressed with the particle bans and placed directly after the gerund of the lexical verb, or after the gerund of the auxiliary räbee in passive causative constructions.

  • Nonor ekki säiki (ormomk) arärä. lit. The man took the woman's killing. = The man was killed by the woman. (take-REDUP-3P man kill-GER [GEN-woman] 3WUM-3WUM.)
  • Ebnüä lisar stiii bans pa sugu rän. lit. I can't take choosing of my house by him. = I can't have my house chosen by him. (DAT-NEG.LOC.COP-1SG DAT-take-GER GEN-choose-GER by 3OUM GEN-house GEN-1SG.)
  • Räb nalul libamt liubatum tärion. lit. Water gives blossoming to flowers. = Water makes flowers blossom. (give-3P water DAT-flower blossom-GER 3NUM-3EUM.)
  • Räb momk äbekki diii sugu mirä. lit. The woman gives a house's choosing to the man. = The woman makes/lets the man choose a house. (give-3P woman DAT-man choose-GER GEN-house 3EUM-3WUM.)
  • Räbeeb'n äbmomk kusoo spamt äbekki näläb. lit. I gave a flower's offering to the man to the woman. = I made/let the woman give a flower to the man. (give-REDUP-1P DAT-woman offer-GER GEN-flower DAT-man 1SG-3SUM.)
  • Äbnorn räbee bans pa siti semikaäbbaba nipa näläb. lit. I will take the giving of ending his task by him. = I will be made by him to end his task. (DAT-take-1P give-GER by 3OUM GEN-end-GER GEN-task GEN-3OUM 1SG-3SUM.)
Impersonal and reflexive expressions

In every clause, the subject must be overtly stated. For impersonal sentences, the demonstrative gä can be used as a generic subject.

  • Lä gä mibee pa. It is daytime. (COP-3P DEM daytime 3OUM.)
  • Elili gä kus papa. A person was seen. / They saw a person. (see-REDUP-3P DEM person 3OUM-3OUM.)
  • Ebnü gä lidotun skä pa. You / One cannot take this. (DAT-NEG.LOC.COP-3P DEM DAT-take-GER GEN-DEM 3OUM.)

The reflexive pronoun usa can be used as a direct or indirect object. It has no SDOAUX form, so reflexive sentences only feature the subject form in the SDOAUX. In the genitive case, it can be helpful in distinguishing possession in the third person. It can also be used after a noun to mean itself, as in something itself.

  • Rättäs usa nä. I taught myself. (teach-1P REFL 1SG.)
  • Elili ugu nipa pion. He saw his (another person's) house. (see-REDUP-3P house GEN-3OUM 3OUM-3EUM.)
  • Elili ugu rusa pion. He saw his (own) house. (see-REDUP-3P house GEN-REFL 3OUM-3EUM.)
  • Edet nid a usa usa läb. The sun itself has died. (end-REDUP-3P sun DEF.ART REFL self 3SUM.)

To reinforce a personal pronoun, if the pronoun is the subject or direct object, it is repeated in the clause in the isolate form, thus existing both in the SDOAUX and the main part of the clause. However, if the personal pronoun is not the subject or direct object, the reflexive usa is used after it as a modifier.

  • Ellikiu kiulu rot olo äbkus ebbisus gä kipa. You yourself know that better than anyone. (know-2P 2SG by good DAT-person DAT-every DEM 2SG-3OUM.)
  • Daäbeenkan äbpa äbsa nä. I talked to him himself. (talk-REDUP-1P DAT-3OUM DAT-REFL 1SG.)

The reflexive pronoun in the dative case is often used adverbially in sentences in which the subject does something to oneself or for one's own benefit, rather than for someone else. It is typically placed directly after the verb.

  • Paba äbsa lugu pa. He went to the house. (go-REDUP-3P DAT-REFL DAT-house 3OUM.)
  • Dononst äbsa bamt nä. I got (myself) a flower. (get-REDUP-1P DAT-REFL flower 1SG.)

Non-finite forms

The word bans for can be used to mean in order to if followed by a gerund, or because if followed by a clause. In the first case, the direct object is in the genitive case, and indirect objects are treated as adverbial phrases, much like the case with locative copula constructions.

  • bans gir lit. for fighting = in order to fight (for fight-GER)
  • Dosuepibil bans iti rän pa. lit. He stayed for my killing. = He stayed in order to kill me. (stay-REDUP-3P for end-GER GEN-1SG 3OUM.)
  • Pabask limimi bans piikusoo spamt äbmomk nä. lit. I went there for the not giving of the flower to the woman. = I went there in order not to give the flower to the woman. (go-REDUP-1P DAT-there for NEG-give-GER GEN-flower DAT-woman 1SG.)

When bans is used to mean because, the main verb is also in the gerund form, but the subject is marked with the word bans, similarly to agents in passive clauses. In such constructions, the subject followed by bans is placed before a potential direct object, which is in the genitive form. In this meaning, the construction can also mean so that something happens.

  • bans gir bans nä lit. for fighting by me = because I fight (for fight-GER for 1SG)
  • Dosuepibil bans ebiti bans nä rusa pa. lit. He stayed for to-dying by me. = He stayed so that I would die. (stay-REDUP-3P for DAT-end-GER for 1SG GEN-self 3OUM.)
  • bans äbsun likusoo bans nebi spamt äbekki lit. for to-being at the giving by us of the flower to the man = because we can give the flower to the man (for DAT-LOC.COP-GER DAT-give-GER by 1SET GEN-flower DAT-man)
  • Peomeom bans piäbeekasun bans nä nipa gä papa. lit. He did it for not having talked about him by me. = He did it because I didn't talk about him. (do-REDUP-3P for NEG-talk-REDUP-GER by 1SG GEN-3OUM DEM 3OUM-3OUM.)

The word äbee with can be used to mean while doing if followed by a gerund, and having done if followed by a perfect (reduplicated) gerund. The form is impersonal by default, but the subject can be specified using bans. All structural rules which apply to the aforementioned bans as in order to or because apply to äbee as well.

  • äbee lipeomu lit. with doing = while doing (with DAT-do-GER)
  • äbee likusoo spamt äb'n lit. with giving of the flower to me = while giving a flower to me (with DAT-give-GER GEN-flower DAT-1SG)
  • Skeget äbee ligir nipa pa. lit. He died with his fighting. = He died while fighting him. (die-REDUP-3P with DAT-fight-GER GEN-3OUM REFL 3OUM.)
  • Skeget äbee lisun ligir bans pa usa pa. lit. He died with being at the fighting by himself. = He died while he (himself) was fighting. (die-REDUP-3P with DAT-LOC.COP-GER DAT-fight-GER by 3OUM 3OUM.)
  • äbee linänäl lugu lit. with having come to the house = having come to the house (with DAT-come-REDUP-GER DAT-house)
  • Gimisdadar äbee lipabage bans nä pa. lit. He stood with having gone by me. = He stood up after I went. (stand-REDUP-3P with DAT-go-REDUP-GER by 1SG 3OUM.)

The word rütnü without can be used to mean without doing if followed by a gerund. The form is impersonal by default, but similarly to äbee, the subject can be marked with bans.

  • rütnü peomu without doing (without do-GER)
  • Edest usa rütnü räbee bans pa skaio äb'n nä. lit. I died without loving by him of me. = I died without him loving me. (end-REDUP-1P self without give-GER by 3OUM GEN-love DAT-1SG 1SG.)

The word rot by can be used to mean by doing, if one does or one should do. It follows the same structural rules as bans, äbee and rütnü. However, if used to mean one should do, it is used clause-initially before a conjugated verb with no additional marking.

  • Doeseli gä rot skimisdar limimi ugu pion. By standing there you can see the house. (can.see-3P DEM by GEN-stand-GER DAT-there house 3OUM-3EUM.)
  • Äbseosk lisar semirn rot spiisäiki bans nä rekki. lit. Through the man's not killing by me you may be sad. = If I don't kill the man you may be sad. (DAT-LOC.COP-2SG DAT-take-GER GEN-sadness by GEN-NEG-kill-GER by 1SG GEN-man.)
  • Rot rättekiu imbi mikoso kiläb. lit. By (the fact) you tell us the truth. = You should tell us the truth. (by tell-2P DAT-1SET truth 2SG-3SUM.)

Word formation

Since the language is head-initial, in compounds which do not agree in class and the class is dictated by the head noun, the ending of the head noun must be moved to the end of the word, using non-concatenative processes:

  • - if the head noun is monosyllabic and ends in V: copy the V to the end of the word
  • - if the head noun is not monosyllabic and ends in V: move the V to the end of the word unless an illegal cluster emerges, in which case copy the V to the end of the word
  • - if the head noun ends in C(s): move the C(s) to the end of the word
  • - break up any remaining illegal clusters with vowels identical in quality to the preceding vowel (insert the vowel as late as possible - Vnskrsk → VnskVrsk)
  • - also break up clusters if they change class, e.g. saek kill W + kus person O → *saekusk O → saekuik murder W

compounds, zero-der, class changing, pos changing, shortenings, affixes for people (er) thing (ness, ity, etc)

Syntax

word order, compound sentences, relative clauses, quotes

The word order is VSO - the verb is always clause-initial and followed directly by the subject. The direct object is typically placed clause-finally, immediately before SDOAUX, though words may be placed earlier in the sentence to show emphasis or to reinforce the association of the DO with the verb, as long as the subject is placed before the direct object. This shift is especially prevalent for phrasal verbs like et usa die, lit. end oneself, and in longer clauses containing subordinate constructions.
