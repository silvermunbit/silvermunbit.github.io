Introduction

This is a personal conlang. Its name is läl'üta lalǘta, which is also the word meaning language. It does not attempt to be naturalistic, it is not set in any fictional world, nor does it have a fictional group of speakers. It is free of loan words and makes no reference to names of countries, people, brands, culture-specific ideas, etc. existing in our world. Its purpose is to express ideas universal to the human experience, rather than to create specialized or technical texts.

As this is a personal language, its "flavor," that is idiomatic expressions, register and anything that in a natural language would be culture-guided is based on my own thoughts, experiences, and worldview. The language is not inspired by any particular language, natural or fictional, though it draws bits and pieces from many.

Although the language is not naturalistic, it features elements such as non-concatenation, proto-forms, grammaticalization and suppletion, in order to make it more interesting. The language is exclusively head initial, VSO and low-context.

Some of the presented examples make use of made-up forms, not actual words.

5. Stress and phonotactics

Word stress is final if the syllable is closed, otherwise penultimate if the syllable is closed, otherwise antepenultimate. Every other preceding syllable receives secondary stress. In case of exceptions, the stress is marked with an acute in the romanization. The exceptions to the stress rule include:

  • - compound SDOAUX, which are always stressed on the final syllable, e.g. arärä ayayá
  • - second person verb forms ending in chu, which are always stressed on the penult, e.g. elikiu elíchu
  • - the word alun áwow̃ child and any words using alun as a diminutive suffix, which are always stressed on the penult, e.g. nikoalun nikwáwow̃
  • - certain short compounds, where stress can be counted as if two words were used, e.g. läl'üta lalǘta (from läl üta lal üta)
  • - certain clipped compounds, kada kadá (from ka darn ka darn)

The maximum syllable shape is CVC, but:

  • - word-initially sP is allowed
  • - word-medially WsP is allowed
  • - word-finally sP WP Ws WsP LN are allowed

Note that instances of word-initial sb sd sg are respelled as sp st sk, e.g. s- + -dark → stark.

6. Umlaut

An affixed i changes the preceding vowel or combination thereof - ä a e o u → e ä i e i, e.g. so- + mika → semika.
An affixed ü changes the preceding vowel or combination thereof - ä a e o u → e ä ü ü ü, e.g. does + -ü → düüsü.
This does not apply when there is no intervening consonant, e.g. rättä + i → rättäi.

Umlaut applies when an inflectional affix is attached to a word, and in SDOAUX (see Class, Number, Case 4. Clause-final auxiliaries). It does not apply between morphemes in compounds or across word boundaries, so articles, demonstratives and adjectives do not trigger umlaut.

Class, Number, Case


1. Class

Verbs, adjectives and nouns are divided into one of five classes. These are similar to grammatical gender, though gender is typically associated with agreement and rigid categorization, while this system involves harmony and semantic associations. Combinations of words within the same class are considered semantically stronger and may be used in idioms and collocations. Each class has a number of possible endings. This system can be referred to as NSWEO.

CLASS EXAMPLES OF ASSOCIATIONS ENDINGS
North (N) cold, dark, bad, calm, quiet, still, lone, weak L LP Ls LN LsP
South (S) hot, bright, good, lively, loud, move, strong B e o
West (W) masculine, end, old, death, short, hear P ä i
East (E) feminine, start, new, young, life, long, say N NP Ns NsP u
Neutral (O) human, nature s sP ü a

Third person pronouns inflect for class, though animate referents are not bound to class by their biological sex. The classes rather suggest certain features or traits, so the same referent may switch class depending on the context, e.g. a good person may be S, an introverted person may be N. Inanimate referents typically stay within one class, though the class may be changed for metaphoric reasons.

There are a few grammatical words belonging to the O class despite their endings, including gä this, lä what and pronouns ending in either of the two.

There are several clipped compounds which retain their class despite clipping, for example kuska this (S because it is a clipped form of kus kago).

2. Number

Nouns and certain personal pronouns inflect for number using another, independent, animacy-based system. The language employs inverse number, meaning each animacy class has an "expected" number which is unmarked and an "unexpected" number which is marked identically across all animacy classes (via reduplication). These classes are independent of NSWEO and each features a different binary number distinction.

  • - set nouns, including nouns which typically come in a specific number, e.g. seasons (4), eyes (2), parents (2), the sun (1). Set nouns are unmarked when occurring in their "set" number and marked when occurring in any other number
  • - animate nouns, including people and animals. They are unmarked when singular and marked when plural
  • - inanimate nouns, encompassing all other nouns. They are unmarked when collective and marked when singulative

The exact function of the collective-singulative system differs depending on the type of the noun.

  • - count nouns are ambiguous to number and the singulative is applied only to explicitly specify one of something, e.g. leüt tree(s) → leüdüt exactly one (usually specific) tree
  • - mass nouns are collective by default and singulative when spefifying a unit, e.g. nalul water → nalulul a glass/bottle/unit of water
  • - abstract terms are collective when referring to them as concepts and singulative when referring to them as single instances, e.g. tukusup murder → Arar tukususup ari. There's been a murder., niko love → Äbpa lipielilin nikogo dieis misan läläb. Our love will never die.

3. Case

There are three cases - direct (nominative + accusative: used for subjects and direct objects), genitive (used for possessives, doubles as ablative) and dative (used for indirect objects, doubles as locative). The genitive and dative cases are also used when certain particles are employed. The cases may be applied to nouns, pronouns and adjectives, the latter agreeing in case with the noun they describe. However, case prefixes are also applied to verbs to form a number of tenses/aspects - the genitive conveys the meaning of past, while the dative conveys the meaning of future/irrealis.

The direct case is unmarked, while genitive and dative marking depends on the animacy of the noun. Animate nouns, non-set personal pronouns, as well as all verbs are marked with one set of prefixes, inanimate nouns are marked with another, and set nouns and pronouns are marked with yet another set. Attributive adjectives take the same prefixes as the nouns they describe. Note that gerund forms are marked with inanimate prefixes when marked for case, since the animate prefixes are used to mark tense/aspect, e.g. peomu doing → äbpeomu going to do (gerund of future form) (AN.DAT-do-GER), lipeomu to/at doing (dative of gerund) (INAN.DAT-do-GER).

4. Clause-final auxiliaries

Each clause ends with an auxiliary word (hereafter referred to as SDOAUX) which encodes the clause's subject and direct object (if there is one), in that order. This is done using pronouns in specific forms. Each pronoun has a default form, used in isolation or in SDOAUX if the clause has no DO. Each pronoun also has a prefixing S form and a suffixing DO form. If the S or DO in the clause is a pronoun, it only occurs in SDOAUX, but if the S or DO is a noun, it occurs in the clause in the direct case and is reinforced in SDOAUX using appropriate pronouns. See Pronouns 1. Personal pronouns and Appendix 1 - SDOAUX

This rule makes word order significant. The word order is VSO - the verb is always clause initial and followed directly by the subject. The DO is typically placed clause-finally, immediately before SDOAUX, though words may be placed earlier in the sentence to show emphasis, as long as the subject is placed before the direct object.

  • Pask nä. I go. (go-1P 1SG.)
  • Tusp nälu. I kill you. (go-1P 1SG-2SG.)
  • Tup ekki arän. The man kills me. (kill-3P man 3WUM-1SG.)
  • Tusp ekki närä. I kill the man. (kill-1P man 1SG-3WUM.)
  • Tup monk alun mion. The woman kills the child. (kill-3P woman child 3EUM-3EUM.)
  • Tup alun monk mion. The child kills the woman. (kill-3P child woman 3EUM-3EUM.)

Some ambiguities may still arise and must be resolved through context, e.g. Tudup kus papa. (kill-REDUP-3P person 3OUM-3OUM.) can mean He/she/it killed the person., or The person killed him/her/it.

SDOAUX are omitted when the verb in the clause is a copula or a locative copula and the subject is a first or second person pronoun, since these verbs conjugate for person. The same is true for SDOAUX which repeat in subsequent clauses - only the final one must be used, provided that the pronouns refer to the same referents.

  • Seon limi. I am here. (LOC.COP-1SG DAT-here.)
  • Ar limi pa. It is here. (LOC.COP-3P DAT-here 3OUM.)
  • Ägä karst, mun nä. I lose, but I learn. (but lose-1P, learn-1P 1SG.)
  • Ortusp monk nion, tusp alun nion. If I kill the woman, I kill the child. (GEN-kill-1SG woman 1SG-3EUM, kill-1SG child 1SG-3EUM.)

There are cases of multiple subjects or direct objects in a clause (x and y). If pronouns are included therein, they are not omitted in favor of SDOAUX. The SDOAUX in such cases always takes the plural (1P and 2P) or marked (3P) form, the NSWEO determined by the first noun mentioned.

  • Äbnorest ä nä, kiulu si. You and me are going to die. (DAT-die-1P and 1SG, 2SG 1PL.)
  • Elilikiu ä kiulu, ekki luoa. You and the man know. (know-2P and 2SG, man 2PL.)
  • Eli äb ekki, monk e leüt, ugu ariri. The man and the woman see the tree and the house. (see-3P and man, woman and tree, house 3WM-3WM.)

Articles


1. Definite article

There is only a definite article, no indefinite article. It does not inflect for number. The article is placed directly after the head noun, before any descriptors. It stems from the demonstrative gä this which is devoiced to kä and further modified based on noun class:

  • - through dissimilation, kä becomes ka after FRONT vowels and class-specific endings are appended
  • - the form is leveled to the SOUTH ko after FRONT vowels
  • - after BACK vowels, class-specific endings are appended, and the form is leveled to the WEST ki except the EAST form kän
  • - an epenthetic neutral vowel a is added at the beginning of the article after certain consonants or clusters

The resultant forms are as follows:

If the preceding word has no class (definite articles may appear directly after negative articles), the NSWO form is used.

The definite article is not used as liberally as in English. It is primarily used:

  • - typically with set nouns and proper nouns or noun phrases, e.g. nid ako the sun, bis ako the world
  • - with question clauses, e.g. Pielilin lilä ko sonläluguu nipa närä. I don't know where he lives. (NEG-know-1P where DEF.ART GEN-live-GER GEN-3OUM 1SG-3WUM.)
  • - in place of possessive pronouns or demonstratives, e.g. surs rän my head → surs aki the head, mikaäbbaba orlu your task → mikaäbbaba ki the task, ugu gän this house → ugu kän the house
  • - when referring to something specific that has previously been mentioned, e.g. Elilin ugu nion. Lälä do ugu kän mion. I saw a house. The house was big. (see-REDUP-1P house 1SG-3EUM. COP-REDUP-3P big house DEF.ART.)
  • - with head nouns of subordinate clauses, e.g. kus aki selii rän the person who sees me (person DEF.ART GEN-see-GER GEN-1SG)
  • - for superlatives when used after adjectives, e.g. ugu kän do the big house (house DEF.ART big), ugu do ki the biggest house (house big DEF.ART)
2. Negative article

The negative article is indefinite and is the equivalent of the English no ~. It stems from the abbreviated gerund of the negative locative copula nü(r) → nü, essentially meaning lacking. Most of its forms mirror the patterns of the definite article:

POST-FRONT POST-BACK POST-C
NSWO no no nüi nẅi an aw̃
E no no nüän nẅaw̃ an aw̃

The negative article is used:

  • - to negate indefinite sentences (those in which the DO is not a pronoun, or a noun followed by a definite article, demonstrative or possessive pronoun)
  • - to form certain indefinite pronouns
  • - to negate adjectives when used after them, e.g. ugu nüän do no big house (house NEG.ART big), ugu do nüi not-big house (house big NEG.ART)

Pronouns


1. Personal pronouns

First and second person pronouns have singular, set and plural forms. The singular is unmarked, while set is marked with the suffix -is stemming from bis all, and plural is marked with the suffix -oa stemming from boa many. The first person plural pronoun stems from a separate root. The genitive forms double as possessive pronouns. Set pronouns are used when referring to a specific, countable group of entities, e.g. a couple, a team, a herd, a council.

Third person pronouns have a marked and unmarked form, agreeing in number with their referents. If the referent is unmarked, so is the the pronoun; if the referent is marked, so is the the pronoun.

There is also a reflexive pronoun. It has no SDOAUX forms. When it is used as the DO, the SDOAUX only features the subject form (see Verbs 8. Impersonal and reflexive expressions).

Nouns

Nouns inflect for case and number, and each is classed into NSWEO, as explained in previous sections. Note that nouns may have more than one meaning, and different meanings may be classed into different animacy categories, resulting in different inflection patterns. Due to zero-derivation, certain words may be both nouns and verbs, or both nouns and adjectives, which also must be taken into account when inflecting them.

Adjectives


1. Attributive and predicative usage

Since the language is exclusively head-initial, adjectives always follow nouns they modify.

Adjectives agree with the nouns they modify in case and number. Case prefixes are applied in agreement with the animacy of the noun.

  • mika olo good thing(s), kus olo good person
  • mikaga ololo a good thing, kugus ololo good people
  • semika solo of good thing(s), nikus rolo of good person
  • limikaga lololo to a good thing, äbkugus äbololo to good people

Contrary to number and case, adjectives do not necessarily have to agree with the noun's class, since each adjective belongs to one of NSWEO. If an adjective does have the same class as the noun it modifies, its ending changes to that of the noun, adhering to the following patterns:

V-final C-final add the C(s) in N to the end of Adj nid + olo → nid olod good sun

This means that if there are two hypothetical adjectives sabo and sabe, both have the same form sabo when used after nouns ending in -o, or sabe if the noun ends in -e. For disambiguation, phrases like ugu sonläl do lit. house of being big = house which is big (house GEN-COP-GER big) can be used.

Adjectives have no predicative forms. They are used with the copula, similarly to nouns. Adjectives used predicatively do not participate in the above-described ending agreement or number agreement.

  • bie ole good light (light good)
  • Lä olo bie läb. Light is good. (COP-3P good light 3SUM.)

  • bie no ole no good light (light NEG.ART good)
  • bie ole no not good light (light good NEG.ART)
2. Comparison

There is no inflection involved with comparison. To express the comparative degree, the compared noun is used in the dative case and placed after the adjective. When there is no compared noun, the demonstrative gä is used. The adjective can be affirmative to express more or negative to express less.

  • ekki do lileüt man bigger than a tree (man big DAT-tree)
  • Lä do nüi bamt lileüt mion. A flower is less big than a tree. (COP-3P big NEG.ART flower DAT-tree 3EUM.)
  • Lälä mikan rütnü läl olo eligä. I want to be better. (COP-1SG nothing without COP-GER good DAT-DEM.)

The superlative is formed by placing the definite article directly after the adjective, whereas normally it would be placed directly after the head noun.

  • iilius spal ki the worst day (day bad DEF.ART)
  • Lä mika mirn an ki imbis ako pa. This is the least sad thing in the world. (COP-3P thing sad NEG.ART DEF.ART DAT-world DEF.ART 3OUM.)

To compare two nouns of equal quality, a similar construction is used as with the comparative degree, but the adjectival phrase contains the affirmative followed by the negative form.

  • Lä bog bog an monk äbekki mion. The woman is as tall as the man. (COP-3P tall tall NEG.ART woman DAT-man 3EUM.)

Adverbs


1. General rules

Adverbs are typically placed between the subject and the direct object. Since sentences are always verb-initial, adverbial phrases are never placed at the beginning of a sentence. Adverbs do not conjugate and do not have NSWEO.

2. Verb and adjective conversion into adverbs

The preposition rot by, through can be placed in front of adjectives to turn them into adverbs, e.g. olo good → rot olo well. While this preposition typically requires the following word to be in the genitive case, there is no case marking if rot is used adverbially in front of adjectives. It can also be placed before verbs, with the meaning of by doing, if one does, one should do (see Verbs 10. Non-finite forms).

3. Adverbs used with adjectives and other adverbs

Adverbs modifying adjectives or other adverbs follow them. To convey excessiveness the word badans overdo, excessive can be used as an adverb, e.g. milk rot badans too small, rot olo rot badans too well. To express insufficiency, a similar strategy is employed, but with the word bamüi underdo, insufficient, e.g. do rot bamüi not big enough, rot olo rot bamüi not well enough.

Another strategy would be to use augmentatives or diminutives, e.g. ugu milkalun a too small house / a house not big enough, Nänäl kugus rot boado paba. lit. People came too mani-ly. = Too many people came. Using diminutives with adjectives or adverbs with positive connotations expresses the idea of a little too, e.g. Lä ugu doalun mion. The house is a little (too) large.

To express sufficiency, the word müidansa enough, just right is used, e.g. do müidansa big enough, rot olo müidansa well enough.

The adverb roba very (from rot way + boa many), and the indefinite pronouns retbis in all ways, completely and rotan not at all can be used both attributively and predicatively.

  • olo roba very good (good very)
  • Sansk rot olo roba pa. He writes very well. (write-3P by good very 3OUM.)
  • sosar sostagbieo retbis completely burnt (GEN-take-GER GEN-burn-GER completely)
  • Lälä mirn retbis. I am completely depressed. (COP-1SG sad completely.)
  • olo rotan in no way good (good nohow)
  • Lä kago rotan müi mikaga gä sab paba. These two things are not at all the same. (COP-3P same nohow near thing-REDUP DEM two 3OM.)

To express the meaning of quite, rather, the word dar or can be placed after an adjective or adverb. This can be done attributively and predicatively. In the latter case the auxiliary verb ba is used and the adjective is used nominally as a quasi-direct object, thus it does not participate in class agreement with the subject noun. The meaning not very can be achieved by negating the verb, but this form cannot be used attributively.

  • mika do dar quite a big thing (thing big or)
  • Ba mienarn mirn dar tarark. Rain is quite sad. (do-3P rain sad or 3NUM-3NUM.)
  • Piiba leüt dans dar mion. The tree is not very far. (NEG-do-3P tree far or 3EUM-3EUM.)

Particles


1. Conjunctions/prepositions

Conjunctions and prepositions constitute one class (hereafter referred to as particles). In fact, the distinction between them and adverbs is only relevant because particles can attach to a verb in certain constructions, e.g. kus aki spage-eli rän lit. the person of my going-with = the person who is going with me (person DEF.ART GEN-go-GER-with GEN-1SG). In place of particles, case marking can be used to indicate the function of a word in a sentence. The genitive shows possession, but also means from, or about. The dative marks indirect objects, but also location and destination. When case prefixes are attached to the ends of verbs, the forms -son and -eli are used invariably.

Verbs


1. Tense/aspect system

The verbal paradigm is a modified version of a proto-system in which verbs may be modified in two ways: by applying case (direct - non-past, genitive - past, dative - future/irrealis) and by applying other strategies (plain form - realis, LOC.COP DAT-GER - imperfective, reduplication - irrealis). Three forms are shifted in the current system: conditional → perfect → potential. The old system may be used in certain solidified expressions or constructions. The current system is as follows:

DIRECT GENITIVE DATIVE
PLAIN present conditional future
LOC.COP DAT-GER imperfect past habitual potential
REDUP perfect past potential necessitative

The forms serve the following functions:

PRESENT present progressive or habitual actions - I do, I am doing
IMPERFECT past progressive actions that have either finished in the past or have lasted until the present - I was doing, I have been doing
PERFECT past perfective actions - I did, I have done
CONDITIONAL conditions - if I do, if I had done
PAST HABITUAL habitual actions which occurred in the past - I used to do, I would do
PAST POTENTIAL past actions which may have happened - I may have done, I could have done, maybe I have done
FUTURE future perfective or progressive actions - I will do, I am going to do, I will be doing, I will have done
POTENTIAL capability or possibility - I can do, I could do, I may do, maybe I will do
NECESSITATIVE necessity - I must do, I have to do

When using constructions with the locative copula, the direct object of the clause is in the genitive case and follows the gerund of the lexical verb directly. This is different from regular sentences in which the direct object is typically clause-final. If the subject in such clauses is a noun, it is placed between the locative copula and the gerund. When the verb has an indirect object and a direct object, the indirect object follows the direct object, acting as an adverbial phrase. However, if the direct object is not stated, case prefixes/particles are attached to the end of the gerund and the object is in the genitive case.

  • Seon lelii. lit. I am at seeing. = I was looking. (LOC.COP-1SG DAT-see-GER.)
  • Seon lelii sugu. lit. I am at looking of a house. = I was looking at a house. (LOC.COP-1SG DAT-see-GER GEN-house.)
  • Ar ekki lelii ormonk arä. lit. The man is at seeing of the woman. = The man was looking at the woman. (LOC.COP-3P DAT-see-GER GEN-woman 3WUM.)
  • Seosk läbekuns-eli rekki. lit. You are at talking-to of the man. = You were talking to the man. (LOC.COP-2SG DAT-talk-GER-to GEN-man.)
  • Ebsis likusoo spamt äbekki. lit. We to-are at giving of a flower to the man. = We can give a flower to the man. (DAT-LOC.COP-1PL DAT-offer-GER GEN-flower DAT-man.)
  • Orseon lidotun semika sugu. lit. I of-am at taking of things from the house. = I used to take things from the house. (GEN-LOC.COP-1SG DAT-take-GER GEN-thing GEN-house.)

3. Copulae

There is a copula and a locative copula. The copula is followed directly by its complement (a noun or an adjective). The locative copula expresses existence and is the equivalent of the English to be (somewhere) and there is (something somewhere). It is also used to indicate possession. Rather than using a verb like to have, the locative copula is used, with its complement being the possessee and the possessor being used in the dative, e.g. Ar mis äb'n. lit. There is time to me. = I have time. (LOC.COP-3P time DAT-1SG.).

The copulae are the only verbs which do not require SDOAUX, unless the subject is in third person. This is because they are also the only verbs to conjugate for person in first and second person. The first person set and plural endings are -eis and -(i)s respectively, and the second person endings are -iis and -oa; these stem from personal pronouns.

5. Negation and interrogation

With the exception of the copulae, verbs are negated with a prefix pi- before vowels and pii- before consonants; this stems from the verb piir differ. The copula is negated by placing its complement in the genitive case, cf. Lä dü ugu mion. A house is a place. (COP-3P place house 3EUM.) and Lä südü bamt mion. A flower is not a place. (COP-3P GEN-place flower 3EUM). This is because the copula stems from the verb meaning stay and the genitive conveys the meaning of staying (away) from. When the complement is an adjective, the inanimate genitive prefix is used. The locative copula is negated with the verb nür.

Verbs are negated only in definite sentences. Sentences with transitive verbs whose DO is not a pronoun or a noun followed by a definite article, demonstrative or possessive pronoun are negated by attaching the negative article to the DO.

The negative prefix precedes any tense/aspect marking, including the conditional form, e.g. Piorpeomk kipa, äbpeoms näpa. If you don't do it, I will. (NEG-GEN-do-2P 2SG-3OUM, DAT-do-1P 1SG-3OUM.). Gerunds of negated verbs function as nouns and case prefixes are therefore placed before the negative prefix, e.g. kus spielii lit. person of not-seeing = person who doesn't see (person GEN-NEG-see-GER).

Questions are marked with a verbal prefix dar- before vowels and da- before consonants; this stems from the verb darn other. The interrogative prefix always precedes the negative prefix, if one is used. The two prefixes always precede tense/aspect prefixes. This prefix is subject to umlaut. The combination of the two in the form däpii can be used as a tag.

6. Imperatives

Imperatives are formed by using the conditional tense. The negative form is used for affirmative imperatives and the affirmative form is used for negative imperatives. The subject of the clause is the recipient of the imperative; if the SDOAUX is kiulu, it is typically omitted.

  • Ornälk limi (kiulu). lit. If you come here. = Don't come here. (GEN-come-2P DAT-here [2SG].)
  • Piortusp spa. lit. If we don't kill him. = Let's kill him. (NEG-GEN-kill-1P 1PL-3OUM.)
  • Ürnü liuu ololo äbpa mionon. lit. If there isn't a good life for him. = May he have a good life. (GEN-NEG.LOC.COP-3P life-REDUP good-REDUP DAT-3OUM 3EM.)
7. Passive and causative voice

Passive and causative voice is expressed with the auxiliaries nors take and räbe give respectively. The verbs are followed by gerunds. In passive constructions, the agent is expressed with the genitive as in He takes the woman's killing. = He is killed by a woman., unless the verb takes any arguments, in which case the agent is expressed with the particle bans and placed directly after the gerund of the lexical verb, or after the gerund of the auxiliary räbe in passive causative constructions.

  • Nonor ekki tipi (ormonk) arärä. lit. The man took the woman's killing. = The man was killed by the woman. (take-REDUP-3P man kill-GER [GEN-woman] 3WUM-3WUM.)
  • Ebnüä lisar stiii bans pa sugu rän. lit. I can't take choosing of my house by him. = I can't have my house chosen by him. (DAT-NEG.LOC.COP-1SG DAT-take-GER GEN-choose-GER by 3OUM GEN-house GEN-1SG.)
  • Räb nalul libamt liubatum tärion. lit. Water gives blossoming to flowers. = Water makes flowers blossom. (give-3P water DAT-flower blossom-GER 3NUM-3EUM.)
  • Räb monk äbekki diii sugu mirä. lit. The woman gives a house's choosing to the man. = The woman makes/lets the man choose a house. (give-3P woman DAT-man choose-GER GEN-house 3EUM-3WUM.)
  • Räbeb'n äbmonk kusoo spamt äbekki näläb. lit. I gave a flower's offering to the man to the woman. = I made/let the woman give a flower to the man. (give-REDUP-1P DAT-woman offer-GER GEN-flower DAT-man 1SG-3SUM.)
  • Äbnorn räbee bans pa siti semikaäbbaba nipa näläb. lit. I will take the giving of ending his task by him. = I will be made by him to end his task. (DAT-take-1P give-GER by 3OUM GEN-end-GER GEN-task GEN-3OUM 1SG-3SUM.)
8. Impersonal and reflexive expressions

In every clause, the subject must be overtly stated. For impersonal sentences, the demonstrative gä can be used as a generic subject.

  • Lä gä mibie pa. It is daytime. (COP-3P DEM daytime 3OUM.)
  • Elili gä kus papa. A person was seen. / They saw a person. (see-REDUP-3P DEM person 3OUM-3OUM.)
  • Ebnü gä lidotun skä pa. You / One cannot take this. (DAT-NEG.LOC.COP-3P DEM DAT-take-GER GEN-DEM 3OUM.)

The reflexive pronoun go can be used as a direct or indirect object. It has no SDOAUX form, so reflexive sentences only feature the subject form in the SDOAUX. In the genitive case, it can be helpful in distinguishing possession in the third person. It can also be used after a noun to mean itself, as in something itself.

  • Tudusp go nä. I killed myself. (kill-REDUP-1P REFL 1SG.)
  • Elili ugu nipa pion. He saw his (another person's) house. (see-REDUP-3P house GEN-3OUM 3OUM-3EUM.)
  • Elili ugu nigo pion. He saw his (own) house. (see-REDUP-3P house GEN-REFL 3OUM-3EUM.)
  • Paba nid ako go lipielilin läb. The sun itself has died. (go-REDUP-3P sun DEF.ART REFL DAT-unknown 3SUM.)

To reinforce a personal pronoun, if the pronoun is the subject or direct object, it is repeated in the clause in the isolate form, thus existing both in the SDOAUX and the main part of the clause. However, if the personal pronoun is not the subject or direct object, the reflexive go is used after it as a modifier.

  • Elilikiu kiulu rot olo äbkus ebbis gä kipa. You yourself know that better than anyone. (know-2P 2SG by good DAT-person DAT-every DEM 2SG-3OUM.)
  • Daäbenkan äbpa äbgo nä. I talked to him himself. (talk-REDUP-1P DAT-3OUM DAT-REFL 1SG.)

The reflexive pronoun in the dative case is often used adverbially in sentences in which the subject does something to oneself or for one's own benefit, rather than for someone else. It is typically placed directly after the verb.

  • Paba äbgo lugu pa. He went to the house. (go-REDUP-3P DAT-REFL DAT-house 3OUM.)
  • Dononst äbgo bamt nä. I got (myself) a flower. (get-REDUP-1P DAT-REFL flower 1SG.)

10. Non-finite forms

The word bans for can be used to mean in order to if followed by a gerund, or because if followed by a clause. In the first case, the direct object is in the genitive case, and indirect objects are treated as adverbial phrases, much like the case with locative copula constructions.

  • bans gir lit. for fighting = in order to fight (for fight-GER)
  • Dosuepeulul bans tipi rän pa. lit. He stayed for my killing. = He stayed in order to kill me. (stay-REDUP-3P for kill-GER GEN-1SG 3OUM.)
  • Pabask limii bans piikusoo spamt äbmonk nä. lit. I went there for the not giving of the flower to the woman. = I went there in order not to give the flower to the woman. (go-REDUP-1P DAT-there for NEG-give-GER GEN-flower DAT-woman 1SG.)

When bans is used to mean because, the main verb is also in the gerund form, but the subject is marked with the word bans, similarly to agents in passive clauses. In such constructions, the subject followed by bans is placed before a potential direct object, which is in the genitive form. In this meaning, the construction can also mean so that something happens.

  • bans gir bans nä lit. for fighting by me = because I fight (for fight-GER for 1SG)
  • Dosuepeulul bans äbpage bans nä lipielilin pa. lit. He stayed for to-dying by me. = He stayed so that I would die. (stay-REDUP-3P for DAT-go-GER for 1SG DAT-unknown 3OUM.)
  • bans äbsun likusoo bans neis spamt äbekki lit. for to-being at the giving by us of the flower to the man = because we can give the flower to the man (for DAT-LOC.COP-GER DAT-give-GER by 1SET GEN-flower DAT-man)
  • Peomom bans piäbenkasun bans nä nipa gä papa. lit. He did it for not having talked about him by me. = He did it because I didn't talk about him. (do-REDUP-3P for NEG-talk-REDUP-GER by 1SG GEN-3OUM DEM 3OUM-3OUM.)

The word eli with can be used to mean while doing if followed by a gerund, and having done if followed by a perfect (reduplicated) gerund. The form is impersonal by default, but the subject can be specified using bans. All structural rules which apply to the aforementioned bans as in order to or because apply to eli as well.

  • eli lipeomu lit. with doing = while doing (with DAT-do-GER)
  • eli likusoo spamt äb'n lit. with giving of the flower to me = while giving a flower to me (with DAT-give-GER GEN-flower DAT-1SG)
  • Paba lipielilin eli ligir nipa pa. lit. He died with his fighting. = He died while fighting him. (go-REDUP-3P DAT-unknown with DAT-fight-GER GEN-3OUM 3OUM.)
  • Paba lipielilin eli lisun ligir bans pa go pa. lit. He died with being at the fighting by himself. = He died while he (himself) was fighting. (go-REDUP-3P DAT-unknown with DAT-LOC.COP-GER DAT-fight-GER by 3OUM 3OUM.)
  • eli linänäl lugu lit. with having come to the house = having come to the house (with DAT-come-REDUP-GER DAT-house)
  • Gimistadar eli lipabage bans nä pa. lit. He stood with having gone by me. = He stood up after I went. (stand-REDUP-3P with DAT-go-REDUP-GER by 1SG 3OUM.)

The word rütnü without can be used to mean without doing if followed by a gerund. The form is impersonal by default, but similarly to eli, the subject can be marked with bans.

  • rütnü peomu without doing (without do-GER)
  • Pabask lipielilin rütnü räbee bans pa seniko äb'n nä. lit. I died without loving by him of me. = I died without him loving me. (go-REDUP-1P DAT-unknown without give-GER by 3OUM GEN-love DAT-1SG 1SG.)

The word rot by can be used to mean by doing, if one does or one should do. It follows the same structural rules as bans, eli and rütnü. However, if used to mean one should do, it is used clause-initially before a conjugated verb with no additional marking.

  • Doeseli gä rot skimistar limii ugu pion. By standing there you can see the house. (can-see-3P DEM by GEN-stand-GER DAT-there house 3OUM-3EUM.)
  • Äbseosk liläl mirn rot spiitipi bans nä rekki. lit. Through the man's not killing by me you may be sad. = If I don't kill the man you may be sad. (DAT-LOC.COP-2SG DAT-COP-GER sad by GEN-NEG-kill-GER by 1SG GEN-man.)
  • Rot rättekiu meis mikauso kiläb. lit. By (the fact) you tell us the truth. = You should tell us the truth. (by tell-2P DAT-1SET truth 2SG-3SUM.)

Word formation

Since the language is head-initial, in compounds which do not agree in class and the class is dictated by the head noun, the ending of the head noun must be moved to the end of the word, using non-concatenative processes:

  • - if the head noun is monosyllabic and ends in V: copy the V to the end of the word
  • - if the head noun is not monosyllabic and ends in V: move the V to the end of the word unless an illegal cluster emerges, in which case copy the V to the end of the word
  • - if the head noun ends in C(s): move the C(s) to the end of the word
  • - break up any remaining illegal clusters with vowels identical in quality to the preceding vowel (insert the vowel as late as possible - Vnskrsk → VnskVrsk)
  • - also break up clusters if they change class, e.g. tup kill W + kus person O → *tukusp O → tukusup murder W
