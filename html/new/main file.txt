INTRODUCTION

This is a personal conlang. It does not attempt to be naturalistic, it is not set in any fictional world, nor does it have a fictional group of speakers. It is free of loan words and makes no reference to names of countries, people, brands, culture-specific ideas, etc. existing in our world. Its purpose is to express ideas universal to the human experience, rather than to create specialized or technical texts.

As this is a personal language, its "flavor," that is idiomatic expressions, register and anything that in a natural language would be culture-guided is based on my own thoughts, experiences, and worldviews. The language is not inspired by any particular language, natural or fictional, though it draws bits and pieces from many.

Although the language is not naturalistic, it features elements such as non-concatenation, proto-forms, grammaticalization and suppletion, so that it is not boring as shit. The language is exclusively head initial, VSO and low-context.

4. Realization and allophony

<N> codas lower high vowels - <iN üN> ew̃ /ɛɰ̃/, <uN> ow̃ /ʌɰ̃/
<r> coda centralizes non-low unrounded vowels - er or ir ur /ɚ/
<ür> is pronounced as /ɥɚ/

/g/ is realized as [ŋ] intervocalically
/s/ is realized as [s̟~θ]
/c/ and /ɟ/ are realized as [c~tɕ] and [ɟ~dʑ]

/ɯ/ is realized as [ɨ~ɯ]
/ʌ/ is realized as [ɐ~ʌ]
/a/ is realized as [a~ä] following HARD consonants and as [a~æ] following SOFT consonants
/aɹ/ is realized as [aɹ~æɹ]

Two semivowels cannot cooccur adjacently within the same syllable, e.g. <iil> ayw → ay.

y is never pronounced when adjacent to <i ü>, and w ẅ are never pronounced when adjacent to <u ü>, within the same syllable, e.g. <itma> iyma → ima, <kupna> kuwna → kuna, <allu> awwu → au, <kuiü> kwiü → kwü → chü, but <kire> chiye.

y is never pronounced after consonants, e.g. <kia> chya → cha.

Phonologically, SOFT consonants can occur before any vowel and semivowel except w, but HARD consonant cannot occur before FRONT vowels and semivowels (e i ü y ẅ).

5. Stress and phonotactics

Word stress is final if the syllable is closed, otherwise penultimate if the syllable is closed, otherwise antepenultimate. Every other preceding syllable receives secondary stress.

Syllable structure is CVC, but:
    - word-initially <sP> is allowed
    - word-medially <LsP NsP> are allowed
    - word-finally <sP> <LP Ls LsP LN> <NP Ns NsP> are allowed

Note that: instances of <sb sd sg> are respelled as <sp st sk>, e.g. <s-> + <-dark> → <stark>.

6. Umlaut

An affixed <i> changes the preceding vowel or combination thereof - <ä a e o u> → <e ä i e i>.
An affixed <ü> changes the preceding vowel or combination thereof - <ä a e o u> → <e ä ü ü ü>.
This does not apply when there is no intervening consonant, e.g. <ka> + <i> → <kai>.

CLASS, NUMBER, CASE

1. Class

Verbs, adjectives, nouns and pronouns are divided into one of five classes. These are similar to grammatical gender, though they entail few instances of agreement and are meant to carry semantic associations. Combinations of words within the same class are considered semantically stronger and may be used in idioms and collocations. Each class has a number of possible endings. This system can be referred to as NSWEO.

CLASS   EXAMPLES OF ASSOCIATIONS                        ENDINGS
North   cold, dark, bad, calm, quiet, still, lone weak  <L LP Ls LN LsP>
South   hot, bright, good, lively, loud, move, strong   <B e o>
West    masculine, end, old, death, short, hear         <P ä i>
East    feminine, start, new, young, life, long, say    <N NP Ns NsP u>
Neutral human, nature                                   <s sP ü a>

Third person pronouns inflect for class, though human referents are not bound to class by their biological sex. The classes rather suggest certain features or traits, so the same referent may switch class depending on the context, e.g. a good person may be S, an introverted person may be N. Non-human referents typically stay within one class, though the class may be changed for stylistic choice.

Since the language is head-initial, in compounds which do not agree in class, the ending of the head noun must be moved to the end of the word, using non-concatenative processes:
    - if the head noun is monosyllabic and ends in V: copy the vowel to the end of the word
    - if the head noun is not monosyllabic and ends in V: move the vowel to the end of the word unless an illegal cluster emerges, in which case copy the vowel to the end of the word
    - if the head noun ends in C(s): move the C(s) to the end of the word
    - break up any remaining illegal clusters with vowels identical in quality to the preceding vowel (insert the vowel as late as possible - Vnskrsk → VnskVrsk)
    - also break up clusters if they change class, e.g. <tup> W + <kus> O → <*tukusp> O → <tukusup> W

2. Number

Nouns and certain pronouns inflect for number using another, independent system. The language employs inverse number, meaning each class has and 'expected' number which is left unmarked and an 'unexpected' number which is marked identically across all classes (via reduplication). The classes are independent of NSWEO and each features a different binary number distinction.

There are three classes:
    - set nouns, including nouns which typically come in a specific number, e.g. seasons (4), eyes (2), parents (2), the sun (1). Set nouns are unmarked when occurring in their 'set' number and marked when occurring in any other number.
    - animate nouns, including people and animals. They are unmarked when singular and marked when plural.
    - inanimate nouns, encompassing all other nouns. They are unmarked when collective and marked when singulative.

The key difference between the latter two systems is that collective nouns are ambiguous to number and may refer to singular referents, and the singulative form is only applied if one wants to explicitly specify exactly one of something. The number system may be represented as follows:

            UNMARKED    MARKED
SET         Set         Non-set
ANIMATE     Singular    Plural
INANIMATE   Collective  Singulative

3. Case

There are three cases - direct (nominative + accusative: subject and direct object), genitive (possessive) and dative (indirect object). Genitive and dative cases are also used when certain adpositions are employed. The cases may be applied to nouns, pronouns and adjectives, which agree in case with the noun they describe. However, case prefixes are also applied to verbs to form a number of tenses/aspects - the genitive conveys the meaning of "past," while the dative conveys the meaning of "future / irrealis."

The direct case is unmarked, while genitive and dative marking depends on the animacy of the noun. Set and animate nouns, as well as all verbs are marked with one set of prefixes, while inanimate nouns are marked with another. Adjectives are marked in agreement with their referents. Note that while verbs are marked the same way as animate nouns, gerunds are inanimate nouns and are marked as such. There is a small number of set nouns (mostly those referring to body parts occurring in pairs) and set personal pronouns which take otherwise non-productive set marking.

The inanimate forms are the default ones used whenever applying the case prefixes to items other than nouns/adjectives/verbs.

4. Clause-final auxiliaries

Each clause ends with an auxiliary word (hereafter referred to as SDOAUX) which conveys the clause's subject and direct object (if there is one), in that order. This is done using pronouns in appropriate forms.

Each pronoun has a default form, used in isolation or in SDOAUX if the clause has no DO. Each pronoun also has a prefixing S form and a suffixing DO form. If personal pronouns are S or DO they are not stated elsewhere in the clause, but if a noun is S or DO, it is stated in the direct case and reinforced in SDOAUX using appropriate pronouns.

This rule makes word order significant. The word order is VSO - the verb is always clause initial and followed directly by the subject. The DO is typically placed clause-finally, immediately before SDOAUX, though words may be placed earlier in the sentence to show emphasis, as long as the subject is placed before the direct object.

SDOAUX are not used when the verb in the clause is a copula or a locative copula and the subject is a first or second person pronoun.

3. Demonstratives

The demonstratives agree with their referents in NSWEO, but not number. They are placed directly after the noun, meaning before adjectives or any other descriptors. While most function only as demonstrative adjectives, the role of the demonstrative pronoun can be filled by the neutral <gä>, as an alternative to third person personal pronouns.

There is no distinction between proximal and distal demonstratives, but the words <müi> "near" and <dans> "far" may be placed directly before the head noun to convey proximity. The demonstratives are derived from a proto-stem <*gä> "this" which is realized as <g(ä)->, to which endings typical to NSWEO are appended. For O, the original stem is preserved and no endings are appended.
