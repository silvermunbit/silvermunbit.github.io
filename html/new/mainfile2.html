Introduction

This is a personal conlang. It does not attempt to be naturalistic, it is not set in any fictional world, nor does it have a fictional group of speakers. It is free of loan words and makes no reference to names of countries, people, brands, culture-specific ideas, etc. existing in our world. Its purpose is to express ideas universal to the human experience, rather than to create specialized or technical texts.

As this is a personal language, its "flavor," that is idiomatic expressions, register and anything that in a natural language would be culture-guided is based on my own thoughts, experiences, and worldviews. The language is not inspired by any particular language, natural or fictional, though it draws bits and pieces from many.

Although the language is not naturalistic, it features elements such as non-concatenation, proto-forms, grammaticalization and suppletion, so that it is not boring as shit. The language is exclusively head initial, VSO and low-context.

Some examples make use of made-up, not actual words.

5. Stress and phonotactics

Word stress is final if the syllable is closed, otherwise penultimate if the syllable is closed, otherwise antepenultimate. Every other preceding syllable receives secondary stress.

Syllable structure is CVC, but:

  • - word-initially sP is allowed
  • - word-medially LsP NsP are allowed
  • - word-finally sP LP Ls LsP LN NP Ns NsP are allowed

Note that: instances of word-initial sb sd sg are respelled as sp st sk, e.g. s- + -dark → stark.

6. Umlaut

An affixed i changes the preceding vowel or combination thereof - ä a e o u → e ä i e i.
An affixed ü changes the preceding vowel or combination thereof - ä a e o u → e ä ü ü ü.
This does not apply when there is no intervening consonant, e.g. ka + i → kai.

Umlaut applies when an inflectional affix is attached to a word and in NSWEO (see Class, Number, Case 4. Clause-final auxiliaries). It does not apply between morphemes in compounds or between word boundaries.

Class, Number, Case


1. Class

Verbs, adjectives, nouns and pronouns are divided into one of five classes. These are similar to grammatical gender, though they rarely involve agreement and are meant to carry semantic associations. Combinations of words within the same class are considered semantically stronger and may be used in idioms and collocations. Each class has a number of possible endings. This system can be referred to as NSWEO.

CLASS EXAMPLES OF ASSOCIATIONS ENDINGS
North cold, dark, bad, calm, quiet, still, lone, weak L LP Ls LN LsP
South hot, bright, good, lively, loud, move, strong B e o
West masculine, end, old, death, short, hear P ä i
East feminine, start, new, young, life, long, say N NP Ns NsP u
Neutral human, nature s sP ü a

Third person pronouns inflect for class, though human referents are not bound to class by their biological sex. The classes rather suggest certain features or traits, so the same referent may switch class depending on the context, e.g. a good person may be S, an introverted person may be N. Non-human referents typically stay within one class, though the class may be changed for stylistic reasons.

Since the language is head-initial, in compounds which do not agree in class and the class is dictated by the head noun, the ending of the head noun must be moved to the end of the word, using non-concatenative processes:

  • - if the head noun is monosyllabic and ends in V: copy the V to the end of the word
  • - if the head noun is not monosyllabic and ends in V: move the V to the end of the word unless an illegal cluster emerges, in which case copy the V to the end of the word
  • - if the head noun ends in C(s): move the C(s) to the end of the word
  • - break up any remaining illegal clusters with vowels identical in quality to the preceding vowel (insert the vowel as late as possible - Vnskrsk → VnskVrsk)
  • - also break up clusters if they change class, e.g. tup kill W + kus person O → *tukusp O → tukusup murder W
2. Number

Nouns and certain pronouns inflect for number using another, independent system. The language employs inverse number, meaning each class has an 'expected' number which is left unmarked and an 'unexpected' number which is marked identically across all classes (via reduplication). The classes are independent of NSWEO and each features a different binary number distinction.

There are three classes:

  • - set nouns, including nouns which typically come in a specific number, e.g. seasons (4), eyes (2), parents (2), the sun (1). Set nouns are unmarked when occurring in their 'set' number and marked when occurring in any other number
  • - animate nouns, including people and animals. They are unmarked when singular and marked when plural
  • - inanimate nouns, encompassing all other nouns. They are unmarked when collective and marked when singulative

The key difference between the latter two systems is that collective nouns are ambiguous to number and may be used with singular referents, and the singulative form is only applied if one wants to explicitly specify exactly one of something. The number system may be represented as follows:

3. Case

There are three cases - direct (nominative + accusative: subject and direct object), genitive (possessive and ablative) and dative (indirect object and locative). Genitive and dative cases are also used when certain adpositions are employed. The cases may be applied to nouns, pronouns and adjectives, the latter agreeing in case with the noun they describe. However, case prefixes are also applied to verbs to form a number of tenses/aspects - the genitive conveys the meaning of past, while the dative conveys the meaning of future/irrealis.

The direct case is unmarked, while genitive and dative marking depends on the animacy of the noun. Animate nouns, non-set personal pronouns, as well as all verbs are marked with one set of prefixes, inanimate nouns are marked with another, and set nouns and pronouons are marked with yet another set. Adjectives are marked in agreement with their referents. Note that gerund forms are marked with inanimate prefixes when marked for case, since the animate prefixes are used to mark tense/aspect.

The inanimate forms are the default ones used whenever applying the case prefixes to items other than nouns/adjectives/verbs.

4. Clause-final auxiliaries

Each clause ends with an auxiliary word (hereafter referred to as SDOAUX) which conveys the clause's subject and direct object (if there is one), in that order. This is done using pronouns in appropriate forms.

Each pronoun has a default form, used in isolation or in SDOAUX if the clause has no DO. Each pronoun also has a prefixing S form and a suffixing DO form. If personal pronouns are S or DO they are not stated elsewhere in the clause, but if a noun is S or DO, it is stated in the direct case and reinforced in SDOAUX using appropriate pronouns.

This rule makes word order significant. The word order is VSO - the verb is always clause initial and followed directly by the subject. The DO is typically placed clause-finally, immediately before SDOAUX, though words may be placed earlier in the sentence to show emphasis, as long as the subject is placed before the direct object.

SDOAUX are typically omitted when the verb in the clause is a copula or a locative copula and the subject is a first or second person pronoun.

Articles


1. Definite article

There is only a definite article, no indefinite article. It does not inflect for number, and its form is based on class and the phonology of the preceding noun. The article is placed directly after the head noun, before any descriptors. It stems from the proto-demonstrative *gä this which is devoiced to kä and further modified based on the phonology of the preceding noun:

  • - through dissimilation, kä becomes ka after FRONT vowels and class-specific endings are appended
  • - the form is leveled to the SOUTH ko after FRONT vowels
  • - after BACK vowels, class-specific endings are appended to kä:
    • - NORTH: kä + l → käl → kä
    • - SOUTH: kä + e → käe → ke
    • - WEST: kä + i → käi → ke
    • - EAST: kä + n → kän
    • - NEUTRAL: kä
    • (N merges O kä, S merges W ke)
  • - epenthetic vowels (same as the preceding vowel, i.e. the final vowel of the noun) are added at the beginning of the article (for the post-FRONT vowel form ko: after all consonants, for post-BACK vowel forms: after clusters, B, s, l)

The resultant forms are as follows:

POST-FRONT POST-BACK
N, O (V)ko (V)ko (V)kä (V)cha
S, W (V)ko (V)ko (V)ke (V)che
E (V)ko (V)ko (V)kän (V)chaw̃
V after C V after clusters, B, s, l

Though the article is spelled as a separate word, its initial vowel affects the realization of the final consonant of the preceding noun.

The definite article is used:

  • - typically with set nouns and proper nouns or noun phrases, for example nid iko the sun, bis iko the world
  • - with question clauses, for example Pielilin lilä ko sonläluguu nipa närä. I don't know where he lives. (NEG-know-1P where DEF.ART GEN-live-GER GEN-3OUM 1SG-3WUM.)
  • - in place of possessive pronouns or demonstratives, for example surs orn my head → surs ukä the head, mikaäbbaba orlu your task → mikaäbbaba kä the task, ugu gän this house → ugu kän the house
  • - when referring to something specific that has previously been mentioned, for example Elilin ugu nion. Lälä do ugu kän. I saw a house. The house was big. (see.REDUP.1P house 1SG.3EUM. COP.REDUP.3P big house DEF.ART.)
  • - for superlatives when used after adjectives, for example ugu kän do (house DEF.ART big) = the big house, ugu do ke (house big DEF.ART) = the biggest house
2. Negative article

The negative article is indefinite and is the equivalent of the English no ~. It stems from the gerund of the negative locative copula nü, essentially meaning lacking. It takes on forms based on, and behaves much like the definite artile:

The negative article triggers umlaut only in the preceding epenthetic vowel, if one is used.

The negative article is used:

  • - to negate indefinite sentences (those in which the DO is not followed by a definite article, demonstrative or possessive pronoun)
  • - to form certain indefinite pronouns
  • - to negate adjectives when used after them, for example ugu nün do (lit. house NEG.ART big) = no big house, ugu do nüe (lit. house big NEG.ART) = not-big house

Pronouns


1. Personal pronouns

While third person pronouns inflect for number much like nouns, first and second person pronouns have singular, set and plural forms. The singular is unmarked, while set is marked with the suffix -is stemming from bis all, and plural is marked with the suffix -oa stemming from boa many. The first person plural pronoun stems from a separate root. The genitive forms double as possessive pronouns.

Set pronouns are used when referring to a specific, countable group of people, e.g. a couple, a team, a council.

There is also a reflexive pronoun. It has no SDOAUX forms. When it is used as the DO, the SDOAUX only features the subject form.

3. Demonstratives

The demonstratives agree with their referents in NSWEO, but not number. They are placed directly after the noun, meaning before adjectives or any other descriptors. While most function only as demonstrative adjectives, the role of the demonstrative pronoun can be filled by the neutral gä, as an alternative to third person personal pronouns. It may be used in impersonal constructions, acting similarly to the English it and one.

There is no distinction between proximal and distal demonstratives, but the words müi near and dans far may be placed directly before the head noun to convey proximity. The demonstratives are derived from a proto-stem *gä this which is realized as g(ä)-, to which endings typical to NSWEO are appended. 4. Indefinite pronouns

Negative pronouns are formed with the negative article. They are used to negate non-definite sentences and are therefore used with affirmative forms of verbs (cf. Elin ugu kän nion. I see the house. → Pielin ugu kän nion. I don't see the house. and Elin ugu nion. I see a house. → Elin ugu nün nion. (lit. I see no house.) I don't see a house.).

The same pronouns have the meaning of some ~ when used with negative forms of verbs. For example:

  • Piipabask linü nä. (lit. I didn't go nowhere.) = I went somewhere.
  • Piidaäben äbkus ünüä semika nüä nä. (lit. I don't talk to no one about nothing.) = I talk to someone about something.
  • Piidaäben äbpa semika nüä nä. (lit. I don't talk to him about nothing.) = I talk to him about something.
  • Piidaäben äbkus ünüä nipa nä. (lit. I don't talk to no one about him.) = I talk to someone about him.

Nouns

Nouns inflect for case and number, and each is classed into NSWEO, as explained in previous sections. Note that nouns may have more than one meaning, and different meanings may be classed into different animacy categories, resulting in different inflection patterns. Due to zero-derivation, certain words may be both nouns and verbs, or both nouns and adjectives, which also must be taken into account when inflecting them.

Adjectives


1. Usage with nouns and as predicate

Since the language is exclusively head-initial, adjectives always follow nouns they modify.

Adjectives agree with the nouns they modify in case and number. However, since each adjective belongs to one of NSWEO, adjectives do not necessarily have to agree with the noun's class. If an adjective does have the same class as the noun it modifies, its ending changes to that of the noun, according to the following patterns:

ADJECTIVE NOUN RULE EXAMPLE
ends in V ends in V Change the V in A to that of N rade + sabo → rade sabe
ends in V ends in C(s) Add the C(s) in N to the end of A rad + sabo → rad sabod
ends in C(s) ends in V add the V in N to the end of A rade + sag → rade sage
ends in C(s) ends in C(s) add the VC(s) in N to the end of A rad + seb → rad sebad, kus + dask → kus daskus

This means that if there are two adjectives sabo and sabe, they will both have the same form sabo when used after nouns ending in -o, or sabe if the noun ends in -e. For disambiguation, phrases like ugu sonlu do house which is big (lit. house of being big) can be used.

Adjectives have no predicative forms. They are used with the copula, similarly to nouns. Adjectives used predicatively participate in the above-described ending agreement, as well as number agreement.

Adejctives have a negative form used in certain constructions described further in this section. The form is created by attatching the negative article directly after the adjective. This is possible because the negative article is otherwise attached directly to the head noun.

2. Comparison

There is no inflection involved with comparison. To express the comparative degree, the compared noun is used in the dative case and placed after the adjective, e.g. ekki do lileüt man bigger than a tree (lit. man big to tree), Lä do nüe bamt lileüt mion. A flower is less big than a tree. (lit. A flower is not-big to tree.) When there is no compared noun, the demonstrative gä is used, e.g. Lälä mika nüä rütnü läl olo logä. I want to be better. (lit. I want to be good to that.)

The superlative is formed by placing the definite article directly after the adjective, whereas normally it would be placed directly after the noun, e.g. iilius spal akä the worst day (lit. day bad the, Lä mika mirn ino kä imbis ikä pa. This is the least sad thing in the world. (lit. This is thing sad not the in the world.)

To compare two nouns of equal quality, a similar construction is used as with the comparative degree, but the adjective is composed of both its affirmative and negative form, e.g. Lä bog bog ünüe monk äbekki mion. The woman is as tall as the man. (lit. Woman is tall tall not to man.)
