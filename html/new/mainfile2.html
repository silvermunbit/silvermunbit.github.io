Introduction

This is a personal conlang. It does not attempt to be naturalistic, it is not set in any fictional world, nor does it have a fictional group of speakers. It is free of loan words and makes no reference to names of countries, people, brands, culture-specific ideas, etc. existing in our world. Its purpose is to express ideas universal to the human experience, rather than to create specialized or technical texts.

As this is a personal language, its "flavor," that is idiomatic expressions, register and anything that in a natural language would be culture-guided is based on my own thoughts, experiences, and worldviews. The language is not inspired by any particular language, natural or fictional, though it draws bits and pieces from many.

Although the language is not naturalistic, it features elements such as non-concatenation, proto-forms, grammaticalization and suppletion, so that it is not boring as shit. The language is exclusively head initial, VSO and low-context.

Some examples make use of made-up forms, not actual words.

N codas lower high vowels - iN üN ew̃ /ɛɰ̃/, uN ow̃ /ʌɰ̃/.
r coda centralizes some vowels - er or ur /ɚ/.
ür is pronounced as /ɥɚ/.
r is realized as [ɚ] after glides.

/g/ is realized as [ŋ] intervocalically.
/s/ is realized as [s̟~θ] (lisped s).
/c/ and /ɟ/ are realized as [c~tɕ] and [ɟ~dʑ].
Onset P are aspirated when stressed. Aspiration before glides results in their devoicing, e.g. poa pwa [pɰ̊a].
Coda /m/ and /n/ assimilate to following obstruents: [m(ʲ)] before /p(ʲ)/, [ɱ(ʲ)] before /v(ʲ)/, [n] before /t d s/, [ɲ] before /c ɟ ç/, [ŋ] before /k ɡ/.

/ɯ/ is realized as [ɨ~ɯ].
/ʌ/ is realized as [ɐ~ʌ].
/a/ is realized as [a~ä] following HARD consonants and as [a~æ] following SOFT consonants.
/aɹ/ is realized as [aɹ~æɹ].

Two semivowels cannot cooccur adjacently within the same syllable, e.g. iil ayw → ay.

y is never pronounced when adjacent to i ü, and w ẅ are never pronounced when adjacent to u ü, within the same syllable, e.g. itma iyma → ima, kupna kuwna → kuna, allu awwu → au, kuiü kwiü → kwü → chü, but kire chiye.

Phonologically, SOFT consonants can occur before any vowel and semivowel except w, but HARD consonant cannot occur before FRONT vowels and semivowels (e i ü y ẅ).

5. Stress and phonotactics

Word stress is final if the syllable is closed, otherwise penultimate if the syllable is closed, otherwise antepenultimate. Every other preceding syllable receives secondary stress.

Syllable structure is CVC, but:

  • - word-initially sP is allowed
  • - word-medially WsP are allowed
  • - word-finally sP WP Ws WsP LN are allowed

Note that: instances of word-initial sb sd sg are respelled as sp st sk, e.g. s- + -dark → stark.

6. Umlaut

An affixed i changes the preceding vowel or combination thereof - ä a e o u → e ä i e i.
An affixed ü changes the preceding vowel or combination thereof - ä a e o u → e ä ü ü ü.
This does not apply when there is no intervening consonant, e.g. ka + i → kai.

Umlaut applies when an inflectional affix is attached to a word and in SDOAUX (see Class, Number, Case 4. Clause-final auxiliaries). It does not apply between morphemes in compounds or across word boundaries.

Class, Number, Case


1. Class

Verbs, adjectives, nouns and pronouns are divided into one of five classes. These are similar to grammatical gender, though they rarely involve agreement and are meant to carry semantic associations. Combinations of words within the same class are considered semantically stronger and may be used in idioms and collocations. Each class has a number of possible endings. This system can be referred to as NSWEO.

CLASS EXAMPLES OF ASSOCIATIONS ENDINGS
North cold, dark, bad, calm, quiet, still, lone, weak L LP Ls LN LsP
South hot, bright, good, lively, loud, move, strong B e o
West masculine, end, old, death, short, hear P ä i
East feminine, start, new, young, life, long, say N NP Ns NsP u
Neutral human, nature s sP ü a

Third person pronouns inflect for class, though human referents are not bound to class by their biological sex. The classes rather suggest certain features or traits, so the same referent may switch class depending on the context, e.g. a good person may be S, an introverted person may be N. Non-human referents typically stay within one class, though the class may be changed for stylistic reasons.

2. Number

Nouns and certain pronouns inflect for number using another, independent system. The language employs inverse number, meaning each class has an "expected" number which is left unmarked and an "unexpected" number which is marked identically across all classes (via reduplication). The classes are independent of NSWEO and each features a different binary number distinction.

There are three classes:

  • - set nouns, including nouns which typically come in a specific number, e.g. seasons (4), eyes (2), parents (2), the sun (1). Set nouns are unmarked when occurring in their "set" number and marked when occurring in any other number
  • - animate nouns, including people and animals. They are unmarked when singular and marked when plural
  • - inanimate nouns, encompassing all other nouns. They are unmarked when collective and marked when singulative

The difference between the latter two systems is that collective nouns are ambiguous to number and the singulative form is applied if one wants to explicitly specify exactly one of something. This also means that mass nouns are unmarked (collective) by default and marked (singulative) when specifying a unit, e.g. a grain of rice, a glass of water. Abstract terms are collective when referring to them as concepts (e.g. murder, love) and singulative when referring to them as single instances (e.g. There's been a murder., Our love will never die.). The number system may be represented as follows:

UNMARKED MARKED EXAMPLE
SET Set Non-set püi a pair of eyes, püii eye, more than two eyes
ANIMATE Singular Plural kus person, kugus people
INANIMATE Collective Singulative bamt flower(s), babamt a flower
3. Case

There are three cases - direct (nominative + accusative: used for subjects and direct objects), genitive (used for possessives, doubles as ablative) and dative (used for indirect objects, doubles as locative). The genitive and dative cases are also used when certain adpositions are employed. The cases may be applied to nouns, pronouns and adjectives, the latter agreeing in case with the noun they describe. However, case prefixes are also applied to verbs to form a number of tenses/aspects - the genitive conveys the meaning of past, while the dative conveys the meaning of future/irrealis.

The direct case is unmarked, while genitive and dative marking depends on the animacy of the noun. Animate nouns, non-set personal pronouns, as well as all verbs are marked with one set of prefixes, inanimate nouns are marked with another, and set nouns and pronouons are marked with yet another set. Adjectives are marked in agreement with their referents. Note that gerund forms are marked with inanimate prefixes when marked for case, since the animate prefixes are used to mark tense/aspect, e.g. peomu doing → äbpeomu going to do (gerund of future form) (AN.DAT-do-GER), lipeomu to/at doing (dative of gerund) (INAN.DAT-do-GER).

The inanimate forms are the default ones used whenever applying case prefixes to items other than nouns/adjectives/verbs.

4. Clause-final auxiliaries

Each clause ends with an auxiliary word (hereafter referred to as SDOAUX) which conveys the clause's subject and direct object (if there is one), in that order. This is done using pronouns in appropriate forms.

Each pronoun has a default form, used in isolation or in SDOAUX if the clause has no DO. Each pronoun also has a prefixing S form and a suffixing DO form. If personal pronouns are S or DO they are not stated elsewhere in the clause, but if a noun is S or DO, it is stated in the direct case and reinforced in SDOAUX using appropriate pronouns.

This rule makes word order significant. The word order is VSO - the verb is always clause initial and followed directly by the subject. The DO is typically placed clause-finally, immediately before SDOAUX, though words may be placed earlier in the sentence to show emphasis, as long as the subject is placed before the direct object. Some ambiguities may still arise and must be resolved through context, e.g. Tudup kus papa. (kill-REDUP-3P person 3OUM-3OUM.) can mean He/she/it killed the person., or The person killed him/her/it.

SDOAUX are typically omitted when the verb in the clause is a copula or a locative copula and the subject is a first or second person pronoun.

Articles


1. Definite article

There is only a definite article, no indefinite article. It does not inflect for number, and its form is based on the phonology of the preceding word. The article is placed directly after the head noun, before any descriptors. It stems from the proto-demonstrative *gä this which is devoiced to kä and further modified based on noun class:

  • - through dissimilation, kä becomes ka after FRONT vowels and class-specific endings are appended
  • - the form is leveled to the SOUTH ko after FRONT vowels
  • - after BACK vowels, class-specific endings are appended, and the form is leveled to the WEST ki except the EAST form which is kän
  • - an epenthetic neutral vowel a is added at the beginning of the article (for the post-FRONT vowel form ko: after all consonants, for post-BACK vowel forms: after clusters, B, s)

The resultant forms are as follows:

POST-FRONT POST-BACK
NSWO (a)ko (a)ko (a)ki (a)chi
E (a)ko (a)ko (a)kän (a)chaw̃
a after C a after clusters, B, s

If the preceding word has no class (definite articles may appear directly after negative articles), the NSWO form is used.

The definite article is not used as liberally as in English. It is primarily used:

  • - typically with set nouns and proper nouns or noun phrases, e.g. nid ako the sun, bis ako the world
  • - with question clauses, e.g. Pielilin lilä ko sonläluguu nipa närä. I don't know where he lives. (NEG-know-1P where DEF.ART GEN-live-GER GEN-3OUM 1SG-3WUM.)
  • - in place of possessive pronouns or demonstratives, e.g. surs rän my head → surs aki the head, mikaäbbaba orlu your task → mikaäbbaba ki the task, ugu gän this house → ugu kän the house
  • - when referring to something specific that has previously been mentioned, e.g. Elilin ugu nion. Lälä do ugu kän. I saw a house. The house was big. (see-REDUP-1P house 1SG-3EUM. COP-REDUP-3P big house DEF.ART.)
  • - for superlatives when used after adjectives, e.g. ugu kän do the big house (house DEF.ART big), ugu do ki the biggest house (house big DEF.ART)
2. Negative article

The negative article is indefinite and is the equivalent of the English no ~. It stems from the abbreviated gerund of the negative locative copula nü(r) → nü, essentially meaning lacking. It takes on forms based on, and behaves much like the definite artile:

POST-FRONT POST-BACK
NSWO (a)no (a)no (a)nüi (a)nẅi
E (a)no (a)no (a)nüän (a)nẅaw̃
V after C V after clusters, T, s

The negative article does not trigger umlaut in the final vowel of the noun.

The negative article is used:

  • - to negate indefinite sentences (those in which the DO is not a pronoun, or a noun followed by a definite article, demonstrative or possessive pronoun)
  • - to form certain indefinite pronouns
  • - to negate adjectives when used after them, e.g. ugu nüän do no big house (house NEG.ART big), ugu do nüi not-big house (house big NEG.ART)

Pronouns


1. Personal pronouns

While third person pronouns inflect for number much like nouns, first and second person pronouns have singular, set and plural forms. The singular is unmarked, while set is marked with the suffix -is stemming from bis all, and plural is marked with the suffix -oa stemming from boa many. The first person plural pronoun stems from a separate root. The genitive forms double as possessive pronouns.

Set pronouns are used when referring to a specific, countable group of people, e.g. a couple, a team, a council.

There is also a reflexive pronoun. It has no SDOAUX forms. When it is used as the DO, the SDOAUX only features the subject form.

2. Interrogatives
WORD MEANING ETYMOLOGY

kuä kwa who what person (kus person + lä → kuläs → ku(l)ä(s) → kuä)

The words lä and kuä belong to the WEST class despite their neutral meaning, because of their endings. In all cases the orthography is more important than semantic meaning in determining the class of a word.

The word sonlä means which when preceded by a noun and whence or of what otherwise. This means that this interrogative, as well as its demonstrative and indefinite counterparts cannot be nominal, only adjectival. To use them as nominal pronouns, the demonstrative gä must be used in conjunction with them, e.g. gä sonlä which, which one.

3. Demonstratives

The demonstratives agree with their referents in NSWEO, but not number. They are placed directly after the noun, meaning before adjectives or any other descriptors. While most function only as demonstrative adjectives, the role of the demonstrative pronoun can be filled by the neutral gä, as an alternative to third person personal pronouns. It may be used in impersonal constructions, acting similarly to the English it and one.

There is no distinction between proximal and distal demonstratives, but the words müi near and dans far may be placed directly before the head noun to convey proximity (in this context müi and dans do not participate in adjective agreement), e.g. müi ekki gii this man, dans ugu gän that house. Nouns

Nouns inflect for case and number, and each is classed into NSWEO, as explained in previous sections. Note that nouns may have more than one meaning, and different meanings may be classed into different animacy categories, resulting in different inflection patterns. Due to zero-derivation, certain words may be both nouns and verbs, or both nouns and adjectives, which also must be taken into account when inflecting them.

Adjectives


1. Usage with nouns and as predicate

Since the language is exclusively head-initial, adjectives always follow nouns they modify.

Adjectives agree with the nouns they modify in case and number. Case prefixes are applied in agreement with the animacy of the noun. For example:

  • mika olo good thing(s), kus olo good person
  • mikaga ololo a good thing, kugus ololo good people
  • semika solo of good thing(s), nikus rolo of good person
  • limikaga lololo to a good thing, äbkugus äbololo to good people

Contrary to number and case, adjectives do not necessarily have to agree with the noun's class, since each adjective belongs to one of NSWEO. If an adjective does have the same class as the noun it modifies, its ending changes to that of the noun, according to the following patterns:

ADJECTIVE NOUN RULE EXAMPLE
ends in V ends in V change the V in A to that of N bie + olo → bie ole good light
ends in V ends in C(s) add the C(s) in N to the end of A nid + olo → nid olod good sun
ends in C(s) ends in V add the V in N to the end of A mika + bis → mika bisa everything
ends in C(s) ends in C(s) add the VC(s) in N to the end of A sir + spal → sir spälir* bad moon

*Adjective agreement endings trigger umlaut.

This means that if there are two adjectives sabo and sabe, they will both have the same form sabo when used after nouns ending in -o, or sabe if the noun ends in -e. For disambiguation, phrases like ugu sonläl do lit. house of being big = house which is big (house GEN-COP-GER big) can be used.

Adjectives have no predicative forms. They are used with the copula, similarly to nouns. Adjectives used predicatively do not participate in the above-described ending agreement or number agreement.

Adejctives have a negative form used in certain constructions described further in this section. The form is created by attatching the negative article directly after the adjective. This is possible because the negative article is otherwise attached directly to the head noun.

2. Comparison

There is no inflection involved with comparison. To express the comparative degree, the compared noun is used in the dative case and placed after the adjective. When there is no compared noun, the demonstrative gä is used. For example:

  • ekki do lileüt man bigger than a tree (man big DAT-tree)
  • Lä do nüi bamt lileüt mion. A flower is less big than a tree. (COP-3P big NEG.ART flower DAT-tree 3EUM.)
  • Lälä mikan rütnü läl olo eligä. I want to be better. (COP-1SG thing NEG.ART without COP-GER good DAT-DEM.)

The superlative is formed by placing the definite article directly after the adjective, whereas normally it would be placed directly after the noun. For example:

  • iilius spal ki the worst day (day bad DEF.ART)
  • Lä mika mirn ano ki imbis ako pa. This is the least sad thing in the world. (COP-3P thing sad NEG.ART DEF.ART DAT-world DEF.ART 3OUM.)

To compare two nouns of equal quality, a similar construction is used as with the comparative degree, but the adjective is composed of both its affirmative and negative form. For example:

  • Lä bog bog anüi monk äbekki mion. The woman is as tall as the man. (COP-3P tall tall NEG.ART woman DAT-man 3EUM.)

Verbs


1. Tense/aspect system

The verbal paradigm is a modified version of a proto-system in which verbs may be modified in two ways: by applying case (direct - non-past, genitive - past, dative - future/irrealis) and by applying other strategies (plain form - realis, LOC.COP DAT-GER - imperfective, reduplication - irrealis). Three forms are shifted in the current system: conditional → perfect → potential. The old system may be used in certain solidified expressions or constructions. The current system is as follows:

DIRECT GENITIVE DATIVE
PLAIN present conditional future
LOC.COP DAT-GER imperfect past habitual potential
REDUP perfect past potential necessitative

The forms serve the following functions:

PRESENT present progressive or habitual actions - I do, I am doing
IMPERFECT past progressive actions that have either finished in the past or have lasted until the present - I was doing, I have been doing
PERFECT past perfective actions - I did, I have done
CONDITIONAL conditions - if I do, if I had done
PAST HABITUAL habitual actions which occurred in the past - I used to do, I would do
PAST POTENTIAL past actions which may have happened - I may have done, I could have done, maybe I have done
FUTURE future perfective or progressive actions - I will do, I am going to do, I will be doing, I will have done
POTENTIAL capability or possibility - I can do, I could do, I may do, maybe I will do
NECESSITATIVE neccessity - I must do, I have to do

3. Copulae

There is a copula and a locative copula. The copula is followed directly by its complement (a noun or an adejctive). The locative copula expresses existence and is the equivalent of the English to be (somewhere) and there is (something somewhere). It is also used to indicate posession. Rather than using a verb like to have, the locative copula is used, with its complement being the possessee and the possessor being used in the dative, e.g. Ar mis äb'n. lit. There is time to me. = I have time (LOC.COP-3P time DAT-1SG.).

The copulae are the only verbs which do not require SDOAUX, unless the subject is in third person. This is because they are also the only verbs to conjugate for person in first and second person. The first person set and plural endings are -eis and -(i)s respectively, and the second person endings are -iis and -oa; these stem from personal pronouns.

Copulae stem from proto-verbs which are no longer in use, at least with their original meanings. The copula stems from läl → l(äl)- *stay and the locative copula stems from seons → s(eons)- *live. 5. Negation and interrogation

With the exception of the copulae, verbs are negated with a prefix pi- before vowels and pii- before consonants; this stems from the verb piir differ. The copula is negated by placing its complement in the genitive case, cf. Lä dü ugu mion. A house is a place. (COP-3P place house 3EUM.) and Lä südü bamt mion. A flower is not a place. (COP-3P GEN-place flower 3EUM). This is because the copula stems from the verb meaning stay and the genitive conveys the meaning of staying (away) from. When the complement is an adjective, the inanimate genitive prefix is used. The locative copula is negated with the verb nür.

Verbs are negated only in definite sentences. Sentences with transitive verbs whose DO is not a pronoun or a noun followed by a definite article, demonstrative or possessive pronoun are negated by attaching the negative article to the DO.

The negative prefix precedes any tense/aspect marking, including the conditional form, e.g. Piorpeomk kipa, äbpeoms näpa. If you don't do it, I will. (NEG-GEN-do-2P 2SG-3OUM, DAT-do-1P 1SG-3OUM.). Gerunds of negated verbs function as nouns and case prefixes are therefore placed before the negative prefix, e.g. kus spielii lit. person of not-seeing = person who doesn't see (person GEN-NEG-see-GER).

Questions are marked with a verbal prefix dar- before vowels and da- before consonants; this stems from the verb darn other. The interrogative prefix always precedes the negative prefix, if one is used. The two prefixes always precede tense/aspect prefixes. The combination of the two in the form dapii can be used as a tag.

6. Imperatives

Imperatives are formed by using the conditional tense. The negative form is used for affirmative imperatives and the affirmative form is used for negative imperatives. The subject of the clause is the recepient of the imperative; if the SDOAUX is kiulu, it is typically omitted.

  • Ornälk limi (kiulu). lit. If you come here. = Don't come here. (GEN-come-2P DAT-here [2SG].)
  • Piortusp spa. lit. If we don't kill him. = Let's kill him. (NEG-GEN-kill-1P 1PL-3OUM.)
  • Ornü liuu ololo äbpa mionon. lit. If there isn't a good life for him. = May he have a good life. (GEN-NEG.LOC.COP-3P life-REDUP good-REDUP DAT-3OUM 3EM.)

Word formation

Since the language is head-initial, in compounds which do not agree in class and the class is dictated by the head noun, the ending of the head noun must be moved to the end of the word, using non-concatenative processes:

  • - if the head noun is monosyllabic and ends in V: copy the V to the end of the word
  • - if the head noun is not monosyllabic and ends in V: move the V to the end of the word unless an illegal cluster emerges, in which case copy the V to the end of the word
  • - if the head noun ends in C(s): move the C(s) to the end of the word
  • - break up any remaining illegal clusters with vowels identical in quality to the preceding vowel (insert the vowel as late as possible - Vnskrsk → VnskVrsk)
  • - also break up clusters if they change class, e.g. tup kill W + kus person O → *tukusp O → tukusup murder W
